World Models

Literature review for the Tiny World Models project

Terminology and taxonomy, prediction patterns and objectives, inputs and outputs, notable models, training and inference cost. Emphasis on small and recent models.

Three kinds of world model

A world model predicts how an environment changes: given a state, and usually an action, it predicts future states, observations, or rewards. It is used for planning, control, training in imagination, or simulation. Three research areas use the term differently:

Research areaPredictsUsed forPrediction spaceSizeExamples
Model-based RLreward / value / next latentplanning and learning a policysmall latentsmall (≤200M, 1 GPU)Dreamer, MuZero, IRIS, TD-MPC2
Generative simulationnext frame (pixels)interactive video / game enginepixels or image tokenslarge (0.5–14B, clusters)GameNGen, Genie, DIAMOND, Cosmos
Representation modelsfuture embeddingself-supervised features and planninglatent, no decodersmall (no pixel output)I-JEPA, V-JEPA 2, DINO-WM

Origin of the term: Schmidhuber 1990 (recurrent prediction networks), Ha and Schmidhuber 2018 (vision + memory + controller; train a policy on imagined rollouts), LeCun 2022 (JEPA: predict in representation space), then "neural game engine" from 2024. The same word now covers control, video generation, and representation learning. This project targets the small end (rows 1 and 3).

Correction (checked against the papers): the prediction space has two values, not three. Dreamer (RSSM) and IRIS predict latents that are trained with a pixel decoder (loss = reconstruction + KL). JEPA and DINO-WM predict latents with no decoder and no reconstruction. So "latent state" versus "representation" is a difference of objective (next slide), not a separate prediction space.

Objective functions: five types

Reconstruction (VAE / ELBO)PlaNet, Dreamer V1–V3
reconstruction + KL. Learns a latent by reconstructing the observation. The latent is trained with a decoder.
Latent self-predictionJEPA, V-JEPA, DINO-WM
‖ predict − stopgrad(target) ‖ in latent space. No decoder and no pixel loss. Needs a method to prevent collapse (EMA target or VICReg).
Autoregressive token predictionIRIS, TWM, Genie, iVideoGPT
Turn each frame into discrete tokens (VQ), then predict the next token: −log p(next token | past, action).
Diffusion / denoisingDIAMOND, GameNGen, Oasis, Cosmos
Denoise the next frame or latent, conditioned on past frames and the action. Best image quality; cost grows with the number of denoising steps.
Value-equivalentMuZero, EfficientZero, TD-MPC2
Predict no pixels, only reward + value + policy (MuZero) or latent consistency + reward + value (TD-MPC2). The model only has to be correct for planning.

Inputs and outputs

Inputs

  • Observations: pixels (64×64 to 720p), proprioception, depth, multi-camera, or text.
  • Actions: given discrete or continuous (Dreamer, MuZero); inferred without labels (Genie); keyboard and mouse (GameNGen, Oasis); text events (Genie-3).
  • Context: a window of past frames or latents (GameNGen uses the last 64 frames and 64 actions).

Outputs (same as the objective)

  • Next observation: RGB frames at some resolution and frame rate (generative models).
  • Next latent state: a small code for training in imagination and learning a policy (Dreamer, IRIS).
  • Reward, value, policy: no pixels (MuZero, TD-MPC2).
  • Future embedding: a representation to plan against (JEPA, DINO-WM).

Output cost order: reward/value < latent < representation < pixels. A cheaper output gives a cheaper model but a less interactive rollout.

Two directions matter for tiny world models: (1) no decoder — predict representations instead of pixels (JEPA and DINO-feature models, mostly Meta); Yann LeCun left Meta in late 2025 to start a world-model company built on this idea. (2) cheaper backbones — state-space (Mamba) models such as DRAMA (7M, laptop-trainable). Both avoid the cost of pixel generation.

Inference cost and how to reduce model size

Inference typeCostExamples
Learned policylowest: one forward pass; the model is used only in trainingDreamer, IRIS, Δ-IRIS
Search / planninghigh per step even when training is cheap; grows with the number of simulationsMuZero (MCTS 50–800), TD-MPC2 (MPPI 512), PlaNet (CEM)
Frame generationper-frame diffusion or token cost; real time only with few stepsGameNGen 20 FPS, DIAMOND 10 FPS, Genie-3 24 FPS; Cosmos and GAIA are offline

Low training cost does not mean low inference cost. MuZero is small but runs an expensive tree search at every step.

Standard ways to make a world model smaller and cheaper

No decoder, predict embeddings — JEPA, DINO-WM, V-JEPA 2.
State-space (Mamba) backbone for long context at lower cost — DRAMA (7M).
Predict in latent space instead of pixels (RSSM) — Dreamer.
Discrete tokens, small transformer — IRIS, Δ-IRIS (4 tokens/frame).
Self-supervised consistency loss to use fewer samples — EfficientZero.
Train in imagination with replay so few real steps are needed (Atari 100k).

Summary

  • The term covers three research areas: model-based RL, generative simulation, and representation models. They predict reward/value, pixels, and embeddings. This project targets the small ones.
  • The objective is the main design choice. "Latent state" versus "representation" is a difference of objective, not a separate prediction space.
  • Training cost ranges over about six orders of magnitude; parameters over about four. Low training cost does not mean low inference cost.
  • Small models can match large ones on sample efficiency. The best results (above-human Atari 100k, Minecraft diamond, robot planning) come from models of 7M–200M parameters on a single GPU. Larger models mainly add image quality and broader domains.
  • Most promising for tiny world models (recent, 2024–2026): no decoder — V-JEPA 2, DINO-WM, DINO-world; cheaper backbones — DRAMA (Mamba, 7M); data-efficient transformers — DeepMind 2025. The open question: which area and benchmark to target.
