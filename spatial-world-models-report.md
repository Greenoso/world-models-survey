> Reference report on **spatial world models** (Fei-Fei Li's "spatial intelligence" framing), recent world models, and the latest evaluation benchmarks. ## 1. Executive summary

Spatial world models are a class of generative models that build a *persistent, geometrically consistent, explorable 3D representation* of an environment from partial inputs (text, one or more images, video, panorama, or a coarse layout), and let humans or agents interact with it. They sit at the center of what Fei-Fei Li calls "spatial intelligence," which she describes as "the scaffolding upon which our cognition is built" and "the frontier beyond language" ([drfeifei.substack.com](https://drfeifei.substack.com/p/from-words-to-worlds-spatial-intelligence)). Li defines world models as "a new type of generative models" whose understanding, reasoning, generation, and interaction with semantically, physically, geometrically, and dynamically complex worlds go "far beyond the reach of today's LLMs," and she gives three essential capabilities: generative, multimodal, and interactive.

The defining technical distinction is representational. Spatial world models commit to explicit or geometry-grounded 3D structure (Gaussian splats, NeRF, meshes, pointmaps), whereas reinforcement-learning latent world models (Dreamer, MuZero) operate on compact, task-oriented latent states with no geometry, and 2D video world models (Sora, Genie) generate coherent frames in pixel space with bounded memory. The practical consequence is *persistence*: a spatial model should not "forget" regions that leave view, whereas video models suffer "severe forgetting" of previously generated scenes ([arXiv:2506.05284](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.05284)).

This report covers the conceptual foundations across five sections: Li's framing of spatial intelligence and world models; definitions and family boundaries for "spatial world model"; the eight comparative dimensions used to characterize each model (definition/role, inputs and outputs, structure, training objectives, data, training stages, model size, training compute); and a cross-cutting analysis of what is and is not disclosed. The central practical finding is that the leading systems disclose very little — parameter counts and training compute are usually unreported — which has direct implications for academic-scale and "tiny" world models. The boundary between video and spatial models is converging, as video models increasingly add explicit 3D memory to gain persistence.

## 2. Fei-Fei Li: spatial intelligence and world models

Fei-Fei Li's essay *"From Words to Worlds: Spatial Intelligence is AI's Next Frontier"* (Nov 10, 2025) is the primary framing source ([drfeifei.substack.com](https://drfeifei.substack.com/p/from-words-to-worlds-spatial-intelligence); World Labs mirror: [worldlabs.ai/blog/from-words-to-worlds](https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/from-words-to-worlds); TIME version: [time.com/7339693/fei-fei-li-ai](https://time.com/7339693/fei-fei-li-ai)).

### Spatial intelligence

Li does not give a single one-line definition but characterizes spatial intelligence through several framings:

- "Spatial Intelligence is the scaffolding upon which our cognition is built."
- "Spatial intelligence represents the frontier beyond language—the capability that links imagination, perception and action."
- On scope: "Spatial intelligence will transform how we create and interact with real and virtual worlds—revolutionizing storytelling, creativity, robotics, scientific discovery, and beyond."

She also describes the *aspiration* for spatially intelligent machines in human terms — creating "with the imagination of a storyteller," interacting "with the fluency of a first responder," "reasoning with the vision of Eratosthenes," and "engineering with the precision of an industrial designer." Note that this storyteller / first-responder phrasing describes aspirational outcomes and is rhetorical; it is *not* the formal list of world-model capabilities, even though some secondary outlets present it as "the three capabilities." The canonical technical three are below.

### World models — definition and the three capabilities

Li defines a world model as:

> "world models, a new type of generative models whose capabilities of understanding, reasoning, generation and interaction with the semantically, physically, geometrically and dynamically complex worlds - virtual or real - are far beyond the reach of today's LLMs."

She gives three essential capabilities:

1. **Generative:** "World models can generate worlds with perceptual, geometrical, and physical consistency."
2. **Multimodal:** "World models are multimodal by design" — processing inputs such as images, videos, depth maps, text instructions, gestures, or actions.
3. **Interactive:** "World models can output the next states based on input actions."

### World Labs, Marble, and the 2D→3D path

World Labs (co-founded by Fei-Fei Li, Justin Johnson, Christoph Lassner, and Ben Mildenhall; founded in early 2024) states its mission as building "foundational world models that can perceive, generate, reason, and interact with the 3D world — unlocking AI's full potential through spatial intelligence," with the vision of "transforming seeing into doing, perceiving into reasoning, and imagining into creating" ([worldlabs.ai/about](https://www.worldlabs.ai/about)).

The product arc illustrates the move from 2D inputs toward persistent 3D worlds. **Marble** (*"Marble: A Multimodal World Model,"* Nov 12, 2025) is described as "a first-in-class generative multimodal world model" that is "massively multimodal," taking "text, images, video, or coarse 3D layouts" and exporting "Gaussian splats, meshes, or videos" with pixel-accurate camera control; it became generally available on Nov 12, 2025 ([worldlabs.ai/blog/marble-world-model](https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/marble-world-model)). The essay separately calls Marble "the first ever world model that can be prompted by multimodal inputs to generate and maintain consistent 3D environments."

**RTFM** (*"RTFM: A Real-Time Frame Model,"* Oct 16, 2025) addresses persistence directly: it "generates video in real-time as you interact with it," "renders 3D scenes generated from a single image," and uses "spatially-grounded frames as a form of spatial memory" so that "you can interact with RTFM forever and the world will never be forgotten." It "models 3D worlds without relying on explicit 3D representations," via "an autoregressive diffusion transformer operating on sequences of frames," running "at interactive framerates using just a single H100 GPU" ([worldlabs.ai/blog/rtfm](https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/rtfm)). This contrast — generated video that "often lose[s] coherence after a few seconds" versus spatial memory for persistence — captures the conceptual bridge from 2D generation to persistent 3D worlds.

## 3. What is a "spatial world model"?

### Common definition

There is no single canonical definition, but a convergent one across industry and surveys is:

> A **spatial / 3D world model** is a generative model that produces a *persistent, geometrically-consistent, explorable 3D representation* of an environment from partial inputs (text, image(s), video, panorama, or coarse layout), and lets humans or agents interact with that environment.

World Labs frames world models around three core functions — to "reconstruct, generate, and simulate 3D worlds; and allow both humans and agents to interact with them" — lifting "whatever input signals are available into a full 3D world" and iteratively refining it ([worldlabs.ai/blog/marble-world-model](https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/marble-world-model)). The recurring defining attributes are:

- **3D structure** — an explicit or geometry-grounded scene representation (Gaussian splats, NeRF, meshes, pointmaps), not just pixels.
- **Geometric / physical consistency** — surfaces, depth, and layout stay coherent across viewpoints.
- **Persistence** — regions are not forgotten when they leave view; revisiting yields the same scene.
- **Interactivity / explorability** — a user or agent can navigate and, in stronger forms, edit or act on the world.

Broad surveys situate these on a spectrum from latent/representation prediction to full environment simulation ([arXiv:2411.14499](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2411.14499); [arXiv:2510.16732](https://arxiv.org/html/2510.16732v1)).

### How it differs from RL latent world models (Dreamer / MuZero)

| | RL latent world models | Spatial / 3D world models |
|---|---|---|
| Representation | Compact **learned latent state**; no explicit geometry | **Explicit 3D geometry** (splats / NeRF / mesh / pointmaps) |
| Purpose | Planning / policy learning ("imagination" rollouts) for control | Generating navigable, persistent worlds for viewing, editing, simulation |
| Output | Predicted reward / value / next-latent (MuZero) or reconstructed latent rollouts (Dreamer) | A 3D scene a human or agent can explore |
| Spatial fidelity | Not required; latent need not be human-interpretable | Central requirement |

Dreamer learns a latent dynamics model and unrolls trajectories *in latent space* ("latent imagination") to train a policy; MuZero learns an *implicit* model predicting only decision-relevant quantities (reward, policy, value) rather than reconstructing observations ([Dreamer v2](https://gbarnier.github.io/papers/dreamer2.pdf); [model-based RL notes](https://deeprlcourse.github.io/course_notes/model-based/)). The key contrast: latent RL world models are task/control-oriented and need no 3D structure, whereas spatial world models make explicit 3D geometry and explorability the deliverable.

### How it differs from 2D video world models (Sora / Genie)

Video world models generate frames autoregressively or via diffusion and are powerful simulators, but their world lives in pixel/frame space, not an explicit 3D representation. DeepMind explicitly contrasts Genie 3 with 3D methods: its worlds are "created frame by frame" rather than relying on "an explicit 3D representation" like NeRFs or Gaussian Splatting; they are navigable "in real time at 24 frames per second" (720p), with "visual memory extending as far back as one minute ago," consistent "for several minutes" ([deepmind.google/blog/genie-3](https://deepmind.google/blog/genie-3-a-new-frontier-for-world-models/)).

The persistence weakness is the defining gap. Because of "limited temporal context window sizes," video world models "maintain scene consistency during revisits" poorly, "leading to severe forgetting of previously generated environments"; the proposed fix is a "geometry-grounded long-term spatial memory" — an explicit 3D store — which is precisely the boundary between a pure video world model and a spatial one ([arXiv:2506.05284](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.05284)).

**Summary distinction:** Sora/Genie-style models are high-fidelity, *temporally* coherent 2D-frame simulators with bounded memory; spatial/3D world models are *geometrically* coherent, persistent 3D scenes that survive revisits and can often be exported or edited. The claim that "persistent 3D beats streamed video" is competitive industry framing, not a neutral technical verdict — and the boundary is converging, as video models add explicit 3D memory ([arXiv:2506.05284](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.05284); *Geometry Forcing*, [arXiv:2507.07982](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.07982v1)). The "spatial vs. video" line is best read as a spectrum of how explicit the 3D representation is, not a hard dichotomy.

### The main technical families

1. **Feed-forward 3D generation (Gaussian splats / NeRF / meshes).** A single network maps input view(s) directly to a 3D representation in one forward pass, with no per-scene optimization. Examples: AnySplat ([arXiv:2505.23716](https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.23716)), F3D-Gaus ([arXiv:2501.06714](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2501.06714)), and Marble, which exports splats, meshes, or videos and internally uses "millions of 3D gaussians" ([worldlabs.ai/blog/marble-world-model](https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/marble-world-model)).

2. **Autoregressive video-to-3D (streaming geometry).** Models that ingest a video/image stream and maintain a continuously updated 3D state, predicting pointmaps/geometry online. Examples: CUT3R, a recurrent transformer with a persistent internal state ([arXiv:2501.12387](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.12387)); VGGT, feed-forward joint prediction of cameras/depth/geometry; and recent streaming systems (LongStream, tttLRM).

3. **Diffusion-based scene generation.** Iterative outpainting/inpainting plus depth/diffusion priors grow an explorable 3D scene from text or one image. Examples: WonderWorld ([arXiv:2406.09394](https://arxiv.org/html/2406.09394v1)), WonderJourney, LucidDreamer, RealmDreamer ([arXiv:2404.07199](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.07199v1)), HoloDreamer ([arXiv:2407.15187](https://arxiv.org/html/2407.15187v1)).

4. **Real-time frame (interactive) world models.** Autoregressive frame generators that respond to user actions in real time — the video end of the spectrum, where spatial fidelity and persistence are the main limitations. Examples: Genie 3 ([deepmind.google/blog/genie-3](https://deepmind.google/blog/genie-3-a-new-frontier-for-world-models/)), Oasis ([decart.ai](https://decart.ai/publications/oasis-interactive-ai-video-game-model)), Odyssey ([TechCrunch](https://techcrunch.com/2025/05/28/odysseys-new-ai-model-streams-3d-interactive-worlds/)).

The strongest cross-cutting trend is that families 3 and 4 are converging into "spatial": video models acquire explicit 3D memory to gain persistence ([arXiv:2506.05284](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.05284); [arXiv:2507.07982](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.07982v1)). "Spatial world model" is increasingly defined precisely by the *addition of persistent, geometry-grounded 3D state* to otherwise-2D generative video models.

## 4. The eight dimensions, explained

This report characterizes each model along eight dimensions. Below, each is defined together with what to look for and the patterns observed across the surveyed models.

### 1. Definition / role

*What it is:* the model's category and intended function — e.g., spatial/3D generative world model (Marble, HunyuanWorld, HY-World 2.0), real-time interactive world model (RTFM, Genie 3, HY-World 1.5/WorldPlay), video-generation world foundation model for Physical AI (NVIDIA Cosmos), or a training-free 3D scene-generation pipeline (WonderWorld, WonderJourney, WonderVerse).

*What to look for:* whether the system produces an explicit, exportable 3D scene or a stream of frames; whether it is action-conditioned (interactive) or generates a static-but-explorable world; and whether it is a trained model or an assembly of frozen pretrained components. *Pattern:* a clear split between (a) industry foundation models aiming at persistent 3D or real-time interaction and (b) academic pipelines that are training-free assemblies of existing models.

### 2. Inputs and outputs

*What it is:* the conditioning signals consumed and the artifacts produced.

*What to look for:* input breadth (single image; text; multi-image; video; panorama; coarse 3D layout; action/keyboard/camera signals) and output type (Gaussian splats, meshes, pointmaps, RGB-D, or streamed video). *Pattern:* the most capable industry systems are "massively multimodal" on input (Marble takes text, images, video, or coarse 3D layouts) and offer multiple export formats (splats, meshes, or video). Real-time interactive models tend to take a single image plus action input and output streamed frames (RTFM, Genie 3). Academic pipelines typically take a single image and/or text and output a 3D representation (point clouds, 3DGS).

### 3. Structure

*What it is:* the architecture and scene representation.

*What to look for:* the generative backbone (diffusion transformer, autoregressive transformer, multi-view diffusion) and the 3D representation (explicit splats/meshes/pointmaps vs. no explicit 3D). *Pattern:* diffusion transformers dominate the interactive/video end (RTFM's "autoregressive diffusion transformer," Cosmos, HunyuanVideo/Voyager backbones); explicit Gaussian-splat or pointmap representations dominate the persistent-3D end. Several systems deliberately avoid explicit 3D (RTFM, Genie 3) and rely on learned spatial memory instead. Academic pipelines often wrap a frozen image/video diffusion model plus a depth estimator plus a 3DGS/NeRF reconstruction step.

### 4. Training objectives

*What it is:* the losses and learning signals.

*What to look for:* diffusion/flow-matching generative objectives, novel-view rendering supervision, depth/geometry supervision, reinforcement-learning rewards (action-following, visual quality), and distillation objectives. *Pattern:* generative video/3D systems are trained with diffusion or flow-matching objectives, often augmented with Diffusion Forcing for autoregressive rollout; the strongest recent pipelines (Cosmos-Predict2.5, HY-World 1.5/2.0) add an RL stage (GRPO-style with quality/action rewards) and a distillation stage for few-step real-time inference. Feed-forward 3D and video-to-3D models use rendering and geometry/depth supervision. Training-free academic pipelines have no learned objective of their own.

### 5. Data

*What it is:* the training corpora.

*What to look for:* scale, source (web video/images, curated panoramas, synthetic/UE-rendered data, real-robot manipulation data), and whether composition is disclosed. *Pattern:* disclosure is uneven. Some systems name datasets and curation pipelines (HunyuanWorld's curated panorama dataset and three-stage captioning pipeline; Voyager's RGB-D datasets including UE-rendered data; EWMBench/embodied models built on AgiBot-World). Industry foundation models (Marble, RTFM, Genie 3) disclose essentially nothing about training data.

### 6. Training stages

*What it is:* the number and nature of distinct training/fine-tuning phases (pretrain, mid-train, fine-tune, post-train/RLHF, distillation).

*What to look for:* whether the pipeline is single end-to-end or an explicit multi-stage curriculum, and whether per-component models are trained separately. *Pattern:* the best-documented systems use elaborate multi-stage pipelines. NVIDIA Cosmos uses progressive resolution/task stages plus post-training; Cosmos-Predict2.5 runs pretrain → domain SFT → cooldown → model merge → RL → distillation. HY-World 1.5 (WorldPlay) uses four stages (pretrain → middle-training → RL → distillation), and HY-World 2.0 trains each component (WorldStereo, WorldMirror, HY-Pano) in its own 3-stage curriculum. By contrast, industry products (Marble, RTFM, Genie 2/3) disclose little — RTFM notes only "end-to-end" training plus distillation; Genie 2 mentions a base model plus a distilled real-time version. Academic pipelines (WonderWorld, WonderJourney, WonderVerse) have *zero* training stages — all weights are frozen pretrained components, and their "stages" are inference-time only.

### 7. Model size

*What it is:* parameter count.

*What to look for:* total parameters, per-component breakdowns, and backbone sizes. *Pattern:* this is the least-disclosed dimension among the headline systems. Marble, RTFM, Genie 2, and Genie 3 report **no parameter count** (the widely cited "11B" for Genie 3 is third-party and unverified; the 11B figure actually belongs to Genie 1). Where sizes are given they come from open-weight efforts: Cosmos spans 4B–14B across families; Cosmos-Predict2.5 is 2B and 14B; HY-World 1.5 backbones are ~8B and ~5B; Voyager's base is ~13B; HY-World 2.0 components range from ~425M to ~80B; CAT3D's multi-view diffusion is ~850M; DimensionX builds on the ~5B CogVideoX. Training-free academic pipelines have no native parameter count — size is whatever frozen components they assemble.

### 8. Training compute

*What it is:* the compute spent to train the model (GPU-hours, iterations, hardware).

*What to look for:* iteration counts, batch sizes, learning-rate schedules, and hardware. *Pattern:* almost universally **undisclosed** at the level of total GPU-hours or FLOPs. Some open-weight reports give partial proxies — Cosmos and Cosmos-Predict2.5 list per-stage iteration counts, batch sizes, and learning rates; CAT3D reports ~1.4M fine-tuning iterations at batch 128; DimensionX lists per-stage step counts. Inference compute is sometimes stated (RTFM: a single H100; Odyssey: H100 clusters at a reported "$1–$2 per user-hour," reporter-stated), but training compute almost never is.

## 5. Cross-cutting analysis

**Model sizes.** The headline industry systems — Marble, RTFM, Genie 2, Genie 3 — disclose no parameter counts at all, and circulating figures (the "11B" Genie 3 number) are third-party and unverified. Reliable sizes come almost entirely from open-weight releases: NVIDIA Cosmos (4B–14B), Cosmos-Predict2.5 (2B / 14B), Tencent's HY-World line (~425M to ~80B across components, ~8B and ~5B interactive backbones, ~13B Voyager base), and academic models like CAT3D (~850M) and DimensionX (~5B base). A pattern emerges: *interactive, real-time spatial/video models cluster in the multi-billion range* but rely on distillation to fit a real-time inference budget (often a single H100), while *feed-forward 3D-generation models can be much smaller* (CAT3D ~850M, and CAT3D's own paper notes it is smaller than competitors at 1.5B–4.3B).

**Training compute.** This is the single least-disclosed dimension. No surveyed system reports total GPU-hours or training FLOPs. The most one finds are per-stage iteration counts, batch sizes, and learning rates in open-weight technical reports (Cosmos, Cosmos-Predict2.5, CAT3D, DimensionX). Inference compute is disclosed more readily than training compute, because it is a product selling point (RTFM on one H100; Odyssey's per-hour cost), but this tells us little about the cost to *build* these models.

**Data.** Disclosure is uneven and correlates with openness. Open-weight efforts often name corpora and curation pipelines (HunyuanWorld's curated panoramas and three-stage captioning; Voyager's RGB-D and UE-rendered data; embodied benchmarks built on AgiBot-World). Closed industry products (Marble, RTFM, Genie 3) disclose essentially nothing about training data — neither scale nor composition.

**Number of training stages.** There is a sharp trimodal split. (1) *Frontier open-weight systems* use elaborate multi-stage pipelines — Cosmos-Predict2.5's pretrain → SFT → cooldown → merge → RL → distillation, and HY-World 1.5's four-stage pretrain → mid-train → RL → distillation — reflecting heavy investment in post-training (RL) and few-step distillation for real-time use. (2) *Closed industry products* describe little beyond "end-to-end training" plus a distillation step for deployment (RTFM, Genie 2). (3) *Academic pipelines* (WonderWorld, WonderJourney, WonderVerse) have *zero* training stages and are training-free assemblies of frozen pretrained models, with only inference-time optimization per scene.

**Spatial vs. video, what this reveals.** Video-oriented world foundation models (Cosmos, the Hunyuan video backbones, Genie) are converging toward spatial behavior by adding explicit 3D memory, RL post-training, and distillation. The spatial-vs-video distinction is therefore better understood as a spectrum of *how explicit the 3D representation is* and *how persistent the world is*, not a hard dichotomy.

**Implications for academic-scale / tiny world models.** Three implications follow. First, the lack of disclosed parameter counts and training compute from frontier systems means academic work cannot easily benchmark "how small is small enough" against the leaders — the comparison set is the open-weight and academic models, where sizes span from ~425M (HY-World components) and ~850M (CAT3D) up through multi-billion backbones. Second, the *training-free pipeline* paradigm (WonderWorld, WonderJourney, WonderVerse) is the most directly accessible at academic scale: it requires no training budget, composing frozen pretrained diffusion, depth, and segmentation models with per-scene 3DGS/NeRF optimization, and it already delivers explorable 3D scenes. Third, the heaviest costs in frontier pipelines are concentrated in *post-training* (RL) and *few-step distillation for real-time inference* — both of which are optional for a research artifact that does not need real-time deployment. A tiny world model can therefore plausibly target the feed-forward-3D or diffusion-scene-generation families, skip RL and distillation, and lean on geometry/rendering supervision and frozen priors, while treating persistent geometry-grounded memory (the converging definition of "spatial") as the core research contribution rather than raw scale.

Ordered spatial/3D first, then interactive/video. For each: the eight dimensions, key results, a verification note, and sources.


### 6.1 Marble (World Labs)
*World Labs (founded by Fei-Fei Li) · Public beta Sept 2025; general availability Nov 12, 2025 (RTFM real-time component announced Oct 2025) · category: Spatial / 3D generative world model*  
[www.worldlabs.ai/blog/marble-world-model](https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/marble-world-model)

- **Definition / role:** Marble is World Labs' first commercial "frontier multimodal world model," billed as a "first-in-class generative multimodal world model" for spatial intelligence. Its role as a spatial world model is to reconstruct, generate, and simulate persistent, navigable 3D worlds (not just 2D video) that both humans and AI agents can explore and interact with. It produces an explicit, exportable 3D scene representation (Gaussian splats / meshes) rather than only frame-by-frame video, which is what distinguishes it from purely video-based world models. The real-time interactive video exploration capability is provided by a companion model, RTFM (Real-Time Frame Model), integrated into Marble. Source: https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/marble-world-model ; https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/rtfm
- **Inputs:** Massively multimodal prompting. Per Marble docs and blog: (1) text prompt; (2) single image; (3) multiple images (multi-image / multi-view sets); (4) 360 degree panorama; (5) video (under 100MB); (6) coarse 3D layout / "3D Structure" authored via the in-app "Chisel" tool (sculpting worlds directly in 3D). The World API additionally lists text, single images, multi-image sets, 360 panoramas, and video as supported inputs. No camera-pose or action-stream input is reported for Marble's authoring flow (RTFM, the real-time component, internally conditions on 3D camera pose). Sources: https://docs.worldlabs.ai/ ; https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/marble-world-model ; https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/announcing-the-world-api
- **Outputs:** Explicit, persistent 3D worlds exportable in several formats. Gaussian splats: native .SPZ and universal .PLY, in two resolution tiers (~2 million splats or ~500k splats). Meshes (GLB): a collider mesh (100-200k triangles, for physics) and high-quality meshes (one ~600k triangles with texture, one ~1M triangles with vertex colors; up to ~1 hour to generate, rate-limited to 4/hour). 360 panorama export: equirectangular PNG at 2560 x 1280. Video output with "pixel-accurate camera control" (real-time interactive frames via RTFM; exact resolution/FPS not reported for Marble). Coordinate system: OpenCV (+x left, +y down, +z forward). Persistence: worlds are persistent and fully navigable (RTFM provides "unbounded persistence" / 3D-consistent exploration). Approx generation times: panorama ~30s (text/image/3D) or ~2 min (multi-image/video); draft ~20s; full world ~5 min; expansion ~5 min; HQ mesh ~1 hour. Sources: https://docs.worldlabs.ai/marble/export/specs ; https://docs.worldlabs.ai/ ; https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/marble-world-model ; https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/rtfm
- **Structure:** Marble's own architecture is NOT disclosed (no paper/model card). World Labs describes it only as "multimodal world models that can reconstruct, generate, and simulate 3D worlds," producing a 3D Gaussian-splatting representation (semitransparent particles) plus derived meshes. The integrated real-time component, RTFM (Real-Time Frame Model), is described as "an autoregressive diffusion transformer operating on sequences of frames," trained end-to-end to predict the next frame conditioned on previous frames; it acts as a learned renderer (2D images in -> new viewpoints out) with no explicit 3D geometry. RTFM's persistence mechanism is "posed frames as spatial memory" (each frame tagged with 3D position/orientation) plus "context juggling" (retrieving nearby frames as context). Specific backbone sizes, tokenizer, layer counts not reported. Sources: https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/rtfm ; https://docs.worldlabs.ai/ ; https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/marble-world-model
- **Training objectives:** For Marble specifically: not reported. For the RTFM real-time component: trained "end-to-end on large-scale video data to predict the next frame conditioned on previous frames" (next-frame prediction within an autoregressive diffusion-transformer framework). Exact loss formulation (e.g., diffusion denoising objective terms) is not detailed. NOTE: the "shortcut forcing" objective sometimes attributed to Marble in secondary blogs is incorrect — that objective and the "Minecraft object-interaction" results belong to DeepMind's Dreamer 4 (arXiv 2509.24527), not Marble. Sources: https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/rtfm ; (mis-attribution traced to https://medium.com/@graison/the-world-model-inflection-2025-made-it-real-f5a9c31475d4 vs https://arxiv.org/abs/2509.24527)
- **Data:** Not reported for Marble/RTFM. Training data is described only as "large-scale video data" with no dataset names, hours, clip counts, scene counts, or real-vs-synthetic breakdown. Source: https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/rtfm (claim verified, unchanged).
- **Training stages:** Not reported. No breakdown of pretrain / fine-tune / post-train / distillation / RLHF stages is published for Marble or RTFM. (Marble is a product built from "multimodal world models"; RTFM is described as end-to-end trained, but staging is not specified.) Sources: https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/marble-world-model ; https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/rtfm
- **Model size:** Not reported / n/r. No parameter count is published for Marble or RTFM. Source: https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/rtfm

### 6.2 RTFM (Real-Time Frame Model)
*World Labs (Fei-Fei Li's company) · 2025-10-16 · category: Generative real-time world model (interactive video / learned renderer for 3D worlds)*  
[www.worldlabs.ai/blog/rtfm](https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/rtfm)

- **Definition / role:** RTFM (Real-Time Frame Model) is a real-time generative World Model from World Labs, released October 16, 2025 as a research preview. Its role as a (spatial) world model: it acts as an end-to-end learned renderer that, given one or more images of a scene, generates new views of that scene in real time as the user interacts with it, modeling both real-world locations (from short videos) and generated/imaginary 3D worlds. Crucially it does NOT use explicit 3D representations (no triangle mesh, no Gaussian splats, no radiance fields/NeRF). Instead it represents the world implicitly via the network's activations and learns geometry, materials, lighting, shadows and reflections directly from large-scale video data. It blurs the line between reconstruction (interpolating between existing views, when many input views are given) and generation (extrapolating new content, when few input views are given). It is designed around three principles: efficiency, scalability, and persistence — worlds remain 3D-consistent and persistent indefinitely no matter how long you interact ("the world will never be forgotten"). (Source: https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/rtfm)
- **Inputs:** One or more 2D RGB images of a scene (a single image, multiple images, or frames from a short video of a real-world location). Each frame is modeled as having a pose (position and orientation) in 3D space, so input is effectively posed 2D image frames. New frames are produced by querying the model with the camera pose of the frame to be generated (the user's interactive viewpoint/navigation provides the target pose). No text, depth/RGBD, action labels, or explicit layout inputs are described. (Source: https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/rtfm)
- **Outputs:** 2D RGB image frames of the scene rendered from new viewpoints, streamed as video in real time ("interactive framerates"). Output is generated autoregressively frame-by-frame conditioned on previous frames and the queried pose. NO explicit 3D output is produced — no Gaussian splats, no mesh, no NeRF/radiance field, no depth/RGBD. Worlds are persistent and 3D-consistent: previously visited locations can be revisited and do not change or disappear. Exact output resolution and a numeric FPS figure are not reported (described only as "real-time"/"interactive framerates"); persistence is described as indefinite/unbounded in principle but bounded in practice by the compute budget. (Source: https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/rtfm)
- **Structure:** An autoregressive diffusion Transformer operating on sequences of frames. Input frames are converted to neural-network activations (the KV cache), which implicitly represent the world; new views are generated via attention over this cache. Each frame carries a 3D pose; generation is conditioned by querying with the target frame's pose. For persistence/long interaction it uses a "spatial memory" of posed frames plus "context juggling" — at each step it retrieves nearby frames from the spatial memory to form a custom (bounded) context for the model, avoiding an ever-growing context. Deliberately NO explicit 3D geometry/representation (no mesh, Gaussian splats, or radiance fields). Exact layer counts, hidden sizes, tokenizer details, diffusion noise schedule, and KV-cache dimensions are not reported. (Source: https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/rtfm)
- **Training objectives:** Trained end-to-end on large-scale video data to predict the next frame conditioned on previous frames (autoregressive next-frame prediction in a diffusion framework). The blog states it "learns to model complex effects like reflections and shadows simply by observing them during training." The specific loss function(s), diffusion objective formulation, and noise schedule are not reported. (Source: https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/rtfm)
- **Data:** "Large-scale video data" used for end-to-end training. No dataset names, number of hours/clips/scenes, or real-vs-synthetic split are reported. The model is shown working on both real-world scenes (from short videos) and generated/imaginary worlds, but the training corpus composition is not disclosed. (Source: https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/rtfm)
- **Training stages:** Not reported as an explicit staged pipeline. (Source: https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/rtfm)
- **Model size:** n/r (no parameter count disclosed). The blog notes only that the current model targets real-time inference on a single H100 GPU and that they "expect larger models targeting larger inference budgets to continue improving." (Source: https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/rtfm)

- **Key results:** Demonstrates a generative world model deployable today on a single H100 GPU at interactive framerates while maintaining persistent, 3D-consistent worlds — a goal World Labs framed against the otherwise extreme compute demands of world models (they note naively rendering 4K@60fps would require >100K tokens/sec, and an hour of persistent interaction >100M tokens of context, deemed infeasible). Renders both real-world scenes (e.g., capturing a glossy piano surface with reflections) and generated/imaginary 3D worlds; unifies reconstruction and generation in one model; achieves persistence via spatial memory + context juggling. Released as an interactive public research preview/demo (https://rtfm.worldlabs.ai). Motivated explicitly by "The Bitter Lesson" and a scalability thesis. No quantitative benchmark metrics (e.g., PSNR/FID/FPS numbers) are reported in the blog. (Source: https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/rtfm)

### 6.5 NVIDIA Cosmos (World Foundation Model Platform)
*NVIDIA · 2025-01-07 (arXiv v1; paper "Cosmos World Foundation Model Platform for Physical AI") · category: Video-generation / predictive World Foundation Model (WFM) platform for Physical AI*  
[arxiv.org/abs/2501.03575](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.03575)

- **Definition / role:** Cosmos is a "World Foundation Model (WFM) Platform" — an end-to-end system to build customized world models for Physical AI (robotics, autonomous driving, simulation). A world model here is a generative simulator that, given a past/current observation and a perturbation (text, action, or future trajectory), predicts plausible future video states of the physical world. It functions as a controllable "data factory" / digital-twin simulator rather than an explicit 3D world model: it is a video-prediction world model (latent video generation) rather than a model that emits an explicit persistent 3D scene representation. Spatial/3D consistency is an emergent property of generated video (evaluated via Sampson epipolar error and camera-pose-estimation success), not an output geometric primitive. The platform ships a video curation pipeline (NeMo Curator), pre-trained WFMs (Diffusion + Autoregressive families), video tokenizers (Cosmos-Tokenizer), guardrails, and post-training recipes for downstream tasks (camera control, robotic/AV action conditioning, multi-view). Open-weight under the NVIDIA Open Model License.
- **Inputs:** Text prompts (natural-language description, recommended <300 words) for Text2World; image or video frame(s) plus optional text for Video2World/Image2World (conditioning on past frames to predict the future); for post-trained/downstream variants: action vectors, camera pose/trajectory (Camera-control / Cosmos-Transfer), and multi-view / robot or AV control signals. Autoregressive variants take text + image + video frames. (Sources: arXiv 2501.03575; HuggingFace model cards.) Not reported: native RGBD or explicit 3D layout/voxel input.
- **Outputs:** RGB video (no explicit 3D/mesh/NeRF/Gaussian-splat output). Diffusion family: 121-frame video clip; default 1280x704 (16:9), also 960x960, 960x704, 704x960, 704x1280; default 24 fps, adjustable 12–40 fps (~5 s at 24 fps). Video2World diffusion outputs 120 future frames conditioned on input. Autoregressive family: next-video-token generation -> up to ~120 future frames at 640x1024 (Video2World 5B/13B); demonstrated streaming at 320x512, 10 fps. The model operates in/produces a continuous latent (8x temporal, 8x8 spatial compression for diffusion) decoded to RGB by the tokenizer. Persistence: no persistent explicit world state; coherence is bounded by generated clip length.
- **Structure:** Two model families plus tokenizers. (1) Diffusion WFM: latent diffusion transformer (DiT-style) operating on continuous tokens from Cosmos-1.0-Tokenizer-CV8x8x8. Blocks of self-attention + cross-attention (text conditioning) + feed-forward MLP, with 3D patchification in latent space, adaptive layer normalization (AdaLN) modulating denoising timestep, and AdaLN-LoRA to cut parameters (~36% reduction); hybrid/3D positional embeddings; T5-XXL text embeddings. (2) Autoregressive WFM: transformer decoder (GPT-style) over discrete video tokens from Cosmos-1.0-Tokenizer-DV8x16x16, with 3D RoPE, cross-attention layers for text conditioning, and QK-normalization for stability. (3) Tokenizers (Cosmos-Tokenizer): continuous variant CV8x8x8 (8x temporal, 8x8 spatial, up to 121 frames) for diffusion; discrete variant DV8x16x16 (8x temporal, 16x16 spatial, FSQ-based, up to 49 frames) for autoregressive. Also: a prompt-upsampler LLM (~12B) and an AR diffusion-decoder (~7B) for refinement. Guardrail models for safety.
- **Training objectives:** Diffusion WFM: EDM denoising score matching — L(D_theta,sigma) = E_{x0,n}[ ||D_theta(x0+n; sigma) - x0||_2^2 ] with per-noise-level uncertainty weighting u(sigma). Autoregressive WFM: standard next-token cross-entropy loss over discrete video tokens (GPT-style). Tokenizer training (Cosmos-Tokenizer): reconstruction objective combining L1 pixel loss ||x_hat - x||_1, VGG-19 perceptual loss, optical-flow consistency loss ||OF(x_hat_t,x_hat_{t-1}) - OF(x_t,x_{t-1})||_1, Gram-matrix (texture/sharpness) loss, and an adversarial/GAN loss added during high-compression fine-tuning; discrete tokenizer uses FSQ (finite scalar quantization). (Source: arXiv 2501.03575 HTML.)
- **Data:** PARTIALLY CONFIRMED. CONFIRMED in primary paper: ~20 million hours of raw video (720p-4k); ~10^8 (~100M) clips for pre-training and ~10^7 for fine-tuning; Physical AI domain mix (driving, robotics/manipulation, human activity/navigation, dynamics/nature, synthetic) — real and synthetic. The paper only cites a narrower ~6.5x transcoding throughput gain. - **Training stages:** Multi-stage. Diffusion WFM: 3 progressive stages — (1) low-res pretraining 512p / 57 frames; (2) high-res pretraining 720p / 121 frames; (3) high-quality fine-tuning at 720p / 121 frames (order of ~10k iterations). Video2World diffusion is post-trained from Text2World with added image/video conditioning. Beyond pretraining: post-training examples for downstream Physical AI tasks (camera control, action/robotic conditioning, multi-view, AV) and an AR diffusion-decoder + prompt-upsampler for refinement. (Sources: arXiv 2501.03575; HuggingFace model cards.)

### 6.6 Cosmos-Predict2.5 / Cosmos-Transfer2.5
*NVIDIA · 2025-10-28 (arXiv v1); v2 dated 2026-02-24 · category: Video world foundation model (world simulator) for Physical AI*  
[arxiv.org/abs/2511.00062](https://arxiv.org/abs/2511.00062)

- **Definition / role:** Cosmos-Predict2.5 is the latest generation of NVIDIA's Cosmos World Foundation Models (WFMs) for Physical AI — a flow-matching video generative model that acts as a "world simulator": it generates high-quality, diverse, physically-coherent visual futures of an environment so embodied agents (robots, autonomous vehicles) can acquire perception/control skills "in silicon" before real-world deployment (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2511.00062, Sec. 1). It is a VIDEO-space (pixel/latent-video) world model, not an explicit 3D/spatial-reconstruction model: it has no 3D Gaussian splat / mesh / NeRF representation; spatial structure is implicit in generated RGB video. It unifies Text2World, Image2World, and Video2World in a single model and serves synthetic-data generation, policy evaluation, and closed-loop simulation. Cosmos-Transfer2.5 is a ControlNet-style extension built on Cosmos-Predict2.5-2B for Sim2Real and Real2Real world translation, conditioned on spatial control inputs (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2511.00062, abstract & Sec. 6.1).
- **Inputs:** Predict2.5: text + image OR text + video (the three modes are Text2World = text only; Image2World = text + 1 reference frame; Video2World = text + multiple conditioning frames, e.g. 5). Conditioning uses a frame-replacement strategy where initial generated frames are substituted with conditioned frames; 0/1/2 or 1/5 conditioning frames are sampled during training (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2511.00062, Sec. 3.2, 4.1). Per HF model card, image input 1280x704 (720p) or 832x480 (480p); video input is 5 frames; text prompt <=300 words (https://huggingface.co/nvidia/Cosmos-Predict2.5-2B). Domain-specialized variants additionally accept actions (robot/action-cond), multi-camera views (7-cam driving; 3-cam robot), and camera pose for camera-controllable multiview. Transfer2.5 inputs: spatial control maps — edges, blurred video, segmentation maps, depth maps (each weighted 0.25 in the uniform multi-control model), plus text; controls may come from a sim engine (NVIDIA IsaacSim) or real video. Driving multiview Transfer conditions on a "world scenario map" (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2511.00062, Tab. 1, Sec. 6.1). NOTE: paper does NOT use RGBD or explicit 3D layout as native inputs for the base model.
- **Outputs:** Output is RGB video (.mp4), NOT 3D Gaussian splats / mesh / NeRF / RGBD. Base model generates 93 pixel frames (= 24 latent frames) at 16 fps, ~5.8 seconds per clip, at up to 720p (1280x704); also supports 256p (320x192) and 480p (832x480) per training stages (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2511.00062, Sec. 3.2, Tab. 4). HF card states 1280x704, 16 FPS, 5-second clip mp4 (https://huggingface.co/nvidia/Cosmos-Predict2.5-2B). Long-horizon video is produced autoregressively in 93-frame chunks (evaluated 30-120s). Multiview driving variant produces 7-camera 720p views; robot variants produce 3-camera views. No persistent 3D scene representation is maintained — temporal/cross-view persistence is achieved implicitly via frame-replacement conditioning and latent-temporal view concatenation (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2511.00062, Sec. 6.1.2, 6.3.1).
- **Structure:** Latent-space Diffusion Transformer (DiT) trained with flow matching, reusing the Cosmos-Predict1 DiT backbone with one change: absolute positional embeddings removed, keeping only relative (3D RoPE) embeddings for resolution/length generalization (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2511.00062, Sec. 3.2). Each DiT block = self-attention + cross-attention (text) + feed-forward MLP (GELU), modulated by adaptive layer norm (scale/shift/gate) with AdaLN-LoRA (dim 256), per timestep. Config (Tab. 3): 2B = 32 layers, model dim 2048, FFN 8192, 16 heads, head dim 128; 14B = 36 layers, model dim 5120, FFN 20480, 40 heads, head dim 128. Visual tokenizer = WAN2.1 VAE (causal VAE), compression 4x8x8 (T,H,W), plus 1x2x2 patchification. Text encoder = Cosmos-Reason1 (a decoder-only Physical-AI VLM) replacing Cosmos-Predict1's T5; activations from multiple blocks per token are concatenated and projected to 1024-dim, fed via cross-attention. Cosmos-Reason1's vision encoder can also accept image/video (left to future work). Transfer2.5-2B follows Cosmos-Transfer1-7B ControlNet design but distributes its 4 control blocks evenly (one after every 7 main-branch blocks) instead of 4 sequentially at the start (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2511.00062, Sec. 6.1).
- **Training objectives:** flow-matching velocity-prediction MSE loss (rectified flow); RL GRPO objective with VideoAlign VLM reward + diffusion-loss regularizer; rCM consistency + distribution-matching distillation loss
- **Data:** CORRECT as stated. Pre-training ~200M curated trainable clips; pipeline processed >200M raw videos / 35M hours raw (up from 20M), yielding >6B clips (5-60s), with ~4% survival under stricter filtering (vs 30% in Predict1) — all verbatim in Sec 2.1. Captions by Qwen2.5-VL-7B (short/medium/long); 26-type taxonomy. Post-train: Robotics (AgiBot-Beta, Bridge, DROID, GR00T, OpenX, RoboMIND); Autonomous Driving ~3.1M 20-second 7-camera surround clips (NVIDIA internal platform); Smart Spaces ~40K clips; Human Dynamics (YOLOX + RTMPose). SFT pool (Tab. 5): Object Permanence 10.4M, High Motion 1.0M, Complex Scenes 1.6M, Driving 3.1M, Robotic Manipulation 730K, plus 388K 4K cooldown videos — all confirmed. Transfer2.5 control data: depth on 10M videos (Video Depth Anything), segmentation on 3M (SAMv2), 14M videos with edge+blur — all confirmed (Sec 6.1). Minor note: the claim lists "1X" among robotics datasets; the paper's enumerated list names AgiBot-Beta, Bridge, DROID, GR00T, OpenX, RoboMIND — "1X" not separately confirmed in this pass, low-impact.
- **Training stages:** Multi-stage progressive pipeline. (1) Pre-training, multi-stage by resolution+task (Tab. 4): Text2Image 256p -> joint Image2World/Video2World 256p -> 480p -> 720p -> add Text2World at 720p (0/1/2 conditioning frames sampled 0.5/0.25/0.25). AdamW, lr 3e-5 (2B) / 1.3e-5 (14B), 2000-step warmup, linear decay. (2) Post-training: (2a) Supervised Fine-Tuning — a SEPARATE model per domain (5 domains), 30k iters, batch 256, plus a 4K-video "cooldown" stage with lr decayed to zero; then MODEL MERGING of the domain SFT + cooldown models (tried model soup, TIES, DARE-Linear, DARE-TIES; >20 merged candidates; model soup chosen). (2b) Reinforcement Learning (GRPO + VideoAlign) on the merged (and pre-trained) 2B model, 256 steps, batch 32; EMA weights released as the final post-train checkpoint. (2c) Timestep distillation (rCM) to a 4-step model (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2511.00062, Sec. 4). So: pretrain -> domain SFT -> cooldown -> merge -> RL -> distillation.
- **Model size:** Two scales: Cosmos-Predict2.5-2B and Cosmos-Predict2.5-14B (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2511.00062, abstract, Tab. 3). Exact 2B count from HF card: 2,059,174,912 parameters (~2.06B) (https://huggingface.co/nvidia/Cosmos-Predict2.5-2B). 14B exact parameter count: not reported numerically in paper (config-only). Cosmos-Transfer2.5-2B is built on the 2B base and is "3.5x smaller" than the 7B Cosmos-Transfer1 (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2511.00062, abstract, Sec. 6.1).
- **Training compute:** Training-efficiency table (Tab. 9) reports runs on 4096 NVIDIA H100 GPUs at 720p / 93 frames: 2B uses context-parallel size 2 with 36.49% MFU; 14B uses context-parallel size 8 with 33.08% MFU. Infrastructure: FSDP2 (hybrid sharded, per-parameter sharding, TorchTitan optimizations), Ulysses-style context parallelism, selective activation checkpointing, NATTEN sparse attention, fused flash-attention with JVP, and an elastic external reward service for RL (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2511.00062, Sec. 4.3, Tab. 9). Human eval: 2B preferred over Wan2.2-5B (30.0% vs 26.2%) and on par with Wan2.1-14B (33.0% vs 34.8%) despite being 60-85.7% smaller; 14B preferred over Wan2.1-14B (48.6% vs 31.8%) and on par with Wan2.2-27B-A14B (38.1% vs 35.9%) at ~half the params (Fig. 6-7). RL post-training raised VideoAlign reward sums substantially (e.g., 2B merged T2W sum 1.23->1.74) and won human votes (Tab. 6, Fig. 5). 4-step distilled 2B nearly matches teacher (T2W overall 0.764 vs 0.768; I2W 0.816 vs 0.810) (Tab. 7-8). Cosmos-Transfer2.5-2B beats Cosmos-Transfer1-7B on control adherence and quality at 3.5x smaller, with far less long-video error accumulation (RNDS). Robot policy: Transfer2.5-augmented diffusion policy scored 24/30 success across 10 test scenarios vs 5/30 (standard aug) and 1/30 (base) (https://arxiv.org/pdf/2511.00062, Tab. 13).

### 6.7 HunyuanWorld 1.0
*Tencent Hunyuan · 2025-07-26 (technical report; arXiv 2507.21809 submitted 2025-07-29) · category: Spatial / 3D world generation model (panorama-proxy, layered 3D mesh / 3DGS scene generation)*  
[arxiv.org/abs/2507.21809](https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.21809)

- **Definition / role:** HunyuanWorld 1.0 is an open-source framework from Tencent Hunyuan for generating immersive, explorable, and interactive 3D worlds from text or image inputs. As a "spatial" world model it is explicitly a world *generation* system rather than an action-conditioned future-state predictor: the report states it "focuses on creating explorable and interactive 3D scenes rather than predicting future states." Its role is to produce a full 360-degree, geometrically consistent, navigable 3D scene (mesh or 3D Gaussian splats) that can be loaded into graphics/game engines, VR, and physics simulators. It positions itself between video-based world generation (rich diversity, no true 3D consistency, costly frame-by-frame rendering, incompatible with CG pipelines) and 3D-based world generation (geometric consistency but scarce data), combining "the best of both worlds" via a semantically layered 3D mesh representation that uses panoramic images as 360-degree world proxies. Three claimed distinguishing features: (1) 360-degree immersive experiences via panoramic world proxies; (2) mesh export for CG pipeline compatibility; (3) disentangled object representations for object-level interactivity. Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.21809 ; https://3d-models.hunyuan.tencent.com/world/HY_World_1_technical_report.pdf
- **Inputs:** Two input modalities: (1) Text conditions (user-provided sentences/prompts; Chinese prompts are first translated and detail-enhanced by an LLM to match the training caption distribution); (2) single Image conditions (a user-provided pinhole/perspective RGB image). For image input, the image is unprojected into panoramic equirectangular (ERP) space using camera intrinsics estimated by a pretrained 3D reconstruction model (MoGe or UniK3D), and an auxiliary scene-aware text description is generated by a VLM to guide completion. No actions, explicit camera-pose control, RGBD, video, or layout maps are taken as user input to the generator (camera trajectories are user-specified only at the downstream rendering/exploration stage via the Voyager video-extension module). Source: https://3d-models.hunyuan.tencent.com/world/HY_World_1_technical_report.pdf (Sec. 2.1)
- **Outputs:** Primary output is a semantically layered, 3D-consistent 3D world. Concretely: (1) a 360-degree equirectangular panorama as an intermediate world proxy; (2) a layered 3D MESH scene built via "sheet warping" with a grid-mesh representation (WorldSheet), decomposed into a sky layer, a background layer, and multiple foreground object layers, with disentangled (separable, interactive) foreground objects supporting translation/rotation/rescaling; (3) optionally 3D GAUSSIAN SPLATTING (3DGS) as an alternative to the mesh, optimized as a layered 3DGS representation based on depth; (4) layer-aligned panoramic DEPTH maps; (5) optional full 3D objects via image-to-3D (Hunyuan3D) generation, and the sky as an HDRI environment map for VR. Meshes are exportable to standard formats for Unity/Unreal/physics engines. Long-range exploration produces world-consistent RGB-D VIDEO via the Voyager module along user-specified camera trajectories. Rendering/eval resolution: perspective views rendered at 960x960 (six or seven views at 90-degree FOV for evaluation). FPS / explicit panorama pixel resolution / persistence duration: not reported (though a 3D point-cloud "world cache" provides cross-clip spatial persistence). Source: https://3d-models.hunyuan.tencent.com/world/HY_World_1_technical_report.pdf (Sec. 2.4-2.5, Sec. 3)
- **Structure:** Staged generative pipeline with three main blocks (Fig. 2). (1) Panorama-DiT: a Diffusion Transformer (DiT) based panorama generation model, built on the Flux text-to-image base model (open-source version uses Flux; method is adaptable to Hunyuan Image, Kontext, Stable Diffusion). A text encoder feeds enhanced prompts; for image-to-panorama, the ERP-projected condition image is encoded by a VAE into latent space and concatenated with the noisy latent. Panorama-specific handling: elevation-aware augmentation in training (random vertical shift with probability p and displacement ratio r) and circular denoising / circular padding with progressive blending at inference for seam continuity. (2) Agentic World Layering: a VLM-driven decomposition (instance recognition, layer decomposition, layer completion) using Grounding DINO (object detection, with circular padding for boundary objects + NMS), ZIM (zero-shot segmentation/matting), and an autoregressive "onion-peeling" inpainting; the Panorama-DiT is fine-tuned for occluded-region completion (PanoInpaint scene model) and a separate sky-completion model is fine-tuned on sky HDRIs (PanoInpaint sky model). (3) Layer-Wise 3D Reconstruction: layer-aligned monocular depth estimation (using MoGe / depth model) with a cross-layer depth-alignment strategy; sheet warping into grid-mesh (WorldSheet) per layer; foreground via direct projection or image-to-3D (Hunyuan3D); background via adaptive depth compression; sky as farthest-depth mesh or HDRI. Optional layered 3DGS. (4) Voyager: a world-consistent video diffusion model with an expandable 3D point-cloud "world caching" mechanism for long-range exploration. System optimizations: XAtlas UV parameterization + mesh decimation (~80% size reduction offline), Draco web compression (~90%), TensorRT + multi-GPU inference acceleration. Source: https://3d-models.hunyuan.tencent.com/world/HY_World_1_technical_report.pdf (Sec. 2)
- **Training objectives:** Not reported as explicit loss equations. The technical report describes the generators as diffusion / Diffusion Transformer models (implying standard diffusion denoising / flow-matching objectives inherited from the Flux base, but the report does not state the loss formula). The layer-completion / inpainting model is trained via "conditional generation of occluded regions" using curated (object-mask, original panorama, object-removed target panorama) triples (an inpainting/reconstruction objective), and a sky-completion model is fine-tuned similarly on sky HDRIs. The cross-layer depth alignment uses a depth-matching criterion that minimizes the distance of overlapped regions across layers (an alignment/consistency objective). No explicit loss function names, weights, or formulas (e.g., L2 diffusion loss, LPIPS, photometric, geometric losses) are given in the report. Source: https://3d-models.hunyuan.tencent.com/world/HY_World_1_technical_report.pdf (Sec. 2.3-2.4)
- **Data:** Training data is panoramic images sourced from three streams: (1) purchased/commercial acquisitions; (2) open-source datasets; (3) custom UE (Unreal Engine) rendered dataset — i.e., a mix of real and synthetic data. A data-curation pipeline applies automated filtering (deduplication, resizing, circular shifting, toxicity detection, quality detection — aesthetics/watermark/clarity, human region/count detection) plus manual expert annotation removing geometric artifacts, visible seams, narrow/unrepresentative scenes, object repetition, anomalous humans, occluded polar regions, distorted/inconsistent geometry. A three-stage captioning pipeline (re-captioning, LLM distillation into varied-length captions, human verification) produces training captions. Auxiliary fine-tuning datasets: panoramic object-removal triples (for scene inpainting) and a sky-HDRI dataset (for sky completion). Concrete dataset scale — number of panoramas/scenes/hours/clips — is NOT reported. Evaluation benchmarks: image-conditioned uses World Labs, Tanks and Temples, and real user images; text-conditioned uses a crowd-sourced prompt benchmark. Source: https://3d-models.hunyuan.tencent.com/world/HY_World_1_technical_report.pdf (Sec. 2.2, Sec. 3.1)
- **Training stages:** No single global "N-stage" pretrain→finetune count is given; the system is trained as a set of components/fine-tunes rather than one end-to-end run. Identifiable training/fine-tuning steps: (1) base Panorama-DiT trained for text-to-panorama and image-to-panorama generation on the curated panorama dataset (with elevation-aware augmentation and circular denoising), built on top of the pretrained Flux base model; (2) fine-tune the Panorama-DiT into a PanoInpaint *scene* completion model on object-removal triples; (3) fine-tune a separate PanoInpaint *sky* completion model on sky HDRIs; (4) a layer-aligned depth model / cross-layer depth alignment for reconstruction (uses pretrained depth models MoGe/UniK3D, plus alignment); (5) the Voyager world-consistent video-diffusion module (separate model, arXiv 2506.04225) for long-range extension. Additionally a three-stage *captioning* data pipeline (re-caption, LLM distillation, human verification). Inference-time efficiency includes TensorRT conversion and selective caching (not a training stage). Source: https://3d-models.hunyuan.tencent.com/world/HY_World_1_technical_report.pdf (Sec. 2.2-2.5); Voyager: https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.04225

### 6.8 HunyuanWorld-Voyager (Voyager)
*Tencent Hunyuan · 2025-06-04 (arXiv v1); open-sourced ~Sept 2, 2025 · category: Spatial/3D world model — explorable 3D scene generation via world-consistent RGB-D video diffusion*  
[arxiv.org/abs/2506.04225](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.04225)

- **Definition / role:** HunyuanWorld-Voyager ("Voyager") is a world-consistent video-diffusion framework that generates explorable 3D scenes from a single image plus a user-defined camera trajectory. As a (spatial) world model, it functions as a controllable, persistent simulator of a 3D environment: starting from one image, it autoregressively extrapolates the surrounding world along arbitrary camera paths while maintaining global geometric/spatial consistency. It jointly produces aligned RGB and depth (RGB-D) video that can be directly unprojected into 3D point clouds, enabling end-to-end scene generation AND reconstruction with inherent cross-frame consistency — eliminating the need for separate post-hoc 3D reconstruction (SfM/MVS). It is positioned as an extension of HunyuanWorld 1.0 that overcomes its occluded-view and limited-exploration-range limitations, advancing "spatial intelligence" for VR, gaming, and robotic simulation. It ranks #1 (average 77.62) on Stanford's WorldScore benchmark. Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.04225 ; https://3d-models.hunyuan.tencent.com/voyager/voyager_en/assets/HYWorld_Voyager.pdf
- **Inputs:** Single RGB image + user-defined camera trajectory (presets: forward/backward/left/right/turn_left/turn_right) + optional text caption; internally warped partial RGB-D + mask. No actions/layout. Source: HYWorld_Voyager.pdf Sec 2.1; GitHub README.
- **Outputs:** Aligned RGB + metric depth (RGB-D) video, 540p, 49 frames/pass; unprojected to 3D point-cloud sequences (export .ply), directly usable for 3DGS; long-range/persistent autoregressive exploration via world cache. FPS n/r. Source: HYWorld_Voyager.pdf; GitHub README.
- **Structure:** DiT (Diffusion Transformer) fine-tuned from HunyuanVideo-I2V: ND double-stream + NS single-stream full-attention blocks operating on VAE latents; channel-wise concat of noisy/partial-RGB/partial-depth/image/mask latents via patch-embed; RGB+depth concatenated along height for depth-fused joint generation; zero-init ControlNet-style control blocks for geometry control; world cache + point culling + smooth video sampling for autoregressive long-range exploration. Source: HYWorld_Voyager.pdf Sec 2.
- **Training objectives:** Latent video-diffusion denoising loss (inherited from HunyuanVideo-I2V): predict/denoise the noisy RGB-D video latents given geometry-injected conditions; control blocks added residually via zero-initialized linear layers. No explicit additional/auxiliary loss equations named in the report. LR 1e-5 (100-step warm-up) decaying to 1e-6. Source: https://3d-models.hunyuan.tencent.com/voyager/voyager_en/assets/HYWorld_Voyager.pdf Sec 2.2 & 3.1
- **Data:** Confirmed correct and quoted verbatim from the primary technical report (Sec on data engine/training data): RealEstate10K = 74,766 real video clips; DL3DV = 3,000 curated high-quality videos segmented into ~18,000 clips; 1,500 Unreal Engine scene models rendered to over 10,000 synthetic video samples; total "over 100,000 video clips." Real + synthetic mix. Camera and depth auto-annotated via VGGT (camera params + depth), MoGE (robust depth estimator), and Metric3D (metric depth scaling). No correction needed.
- **Training stages:** Three stages: (1) RGB-only video model on all 3 datasets; (2) add depth (RGB-D fusion), drop DL3DV; (3) freeze DiT, train only ControlNet/control blocks on UE data with GT depth. No distillation/RLHF. Source: HYWorld_Voyager.pdf Sec 3.1

### 6.9 HY-World 2.0
*Tencent Hunyuan (Team HY-World) · 2026-04-15 (arXiv submission; project/code release April 2026) · category: 3D-native (spatial) world model — text/image/video to persistent navigable 3D world*  
[arxiv.org/abs/2604.14268](https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.14268)

- **Definition / role:** HY-World 2.0 is a multi-modal world model for reconstructing, generating, and simulating 3D worlds, advancing the team's prior HY-World 1.0. Unlike video-based ("pixel/ephemeral") world models such as Genie 3, Cosmos, or HY-World 1.5, it is "3D-first": its role as a spatial world model is to build a persistent, explicit 3D representation of a scene (3D Gaussian Splatting / mesh / point cloud) that is geometrically consistent, editable, and re-renderable in real time ("Build a world, keep it forever"). It serves two functions: world GENERATION (text or single image -> navigable 3D world via a four-stage pipeline) and world RECONSTRUCTION (multi-view images / casual video -> 3D geometry). Output assets are engine-compatible (Blender, Unreal, Unity, Isaac Sim) for downstream simulation. The generation pipeline has four stages: (1) Panorama Generation (HY-Pano 2.0) to initialize the world, (2) Trajectory Planning (WorldNav) to derive exploration camera paths, (3) memory-driven World Expansion (WorldStereo 2.0), (4) World Composition (WorldMirror 2.0 + per-scene 3DGS optimization). Sources: https://arxiv.org/abs/2604.14268 ; https://github.com/Tencent-Hunyuan/HY-World-2.0 ; https://3d-models.hunyuan.tencent.com/world/world2_0/HY_World_2_0.pdf
- **Inputs:** Multi-modal inputs (https://github.com/Tencent-Hunyuan/HY-World-2.0): for generation — text prompts OR a single-view image (HY-Pano 2.0 converts these to a 360 deg ERP panorama). Internally the pipeline also consumes planned camera trajectories/poses (WorldNav, VLM-guided, obstacle-aware). For reconstruction — multi-view images OR casual/monocular video sequences (WorldMirror 2.0). WorldMirror 2.0 additionally accepts OPTIONAL geometric priors that can be toggled on/off: camera extrinsics/poses, calibrated camera intrinsics, and depth maps ("Any-Prior" prompting; each prior randomly toggled with prob 0.5 during training). Source for priors: https://arxiv.org/html/2510.10726v1
- **Outputs:** Persistent, editable 3D assets rather than video frames (https://huggingface.co/tencent/HY-World-2.0): 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) scenes, meshes, dense point clouds, per-pixel depth maps, surface normals, and camera parameters (intrinsics + extrinsics). HY-Pano 2.0 outputs 360 deg ERP panoramic images. WorldMirror 2.0 simultaneously predicts point maps, multi-view depth, normals, camera params and pixel-wise 3DGS attributes in a single feed-forward pass, with flexible-resolution inference (~50K–500K pixels). Persistence: the 3D world is explicit and permanent — one-time generation cost, near-zero rendering cost, real-time rendering on consumer GPUs; assets importable to Blender/Unreal/Unity/Isaac Sim. Specific output FPS not reported (real-time rendering claimed but no exact FPS number given). Resolution given as pixel-budget (50K–500K px) rather than a fixed WxH.
- **Structure:** Modular four-component architecture (https://3d-models.hunyuan.tencent.com/world/world2_0/HY_World_2_0.pdf): (1) HY-Pano 2.0 — a Multi-Modal Diffusion Transformer (MMDiT); conditional image latent is concatenated with panoramic noise latent as a unified token sequence so self-attention learns the perspective->ERP transform; circular padding + pixel blending remove ERP boundary seams. Two backends: HunyuanImage-3 (full think->recaption->diffuse reasoning pipeline) and a Qwen-Image-Edit + LoRA variant. (2) WorldNav — VLM-guided trajectory planner (uses Qwen3-VL for semantic parsing; SAM3 for segmentation; MoGe2 for depth) producing obstacle-aware camera paths. (3) WorldStereo 2.0 — built on a pre-trained video DiT with a camera adapter trained from scratch and a spatial-only Keyframe-VAE; adds memory mechanisms (Global/GGM + SSM++) for multi-view "memory-guided consistency". (4) WorldMirror 2.0 — a unified feed-forward transformer reconstruction model built on VGGT with alternating (global-local) attention; "Any-Modal/Any-Prior Tokenization" (MLPs map pose/intrinsics priors to tokens, conv layers map depth priors to tokens); four DPT prediction heads: DPT_p (point maps), DPT_d (depth), DPT_n (normals), DPT_g (Gaussians). A final per-scene 3DGS optimization (custom gsplat) composes the world. Implementation: PyTorch, BF16, FSDP, FlashAttention-2/3, CUDA 12.8.
- **Training objectives:** Per component (https://3d-models.hunyuan.tencent.com/world/world2_0/HY_World_2_0.pdf ; https://arxiv.org/html/2510.10726v1): HY-Pano 2.0 — diffusion / flow-matching denoising objective of the MMDiT (specific flow-matching loss form not separately detailed in report). WorldStereo 2.0 — diffusion training objective; post-training uses Distribution Matching Distillation (DMD), grad: ∇L_DMD = −E_t[ ∫ (s_real(x_t,t) − s_fake(x_t,t)) dx_t/dθ dz ]; GAN loss explicitly omitted ("impact insignificant while substantially slowing training"). WorldMirror 2.0 — composite multi-task loss: L_points (gradient-based point-map supervision with uncertainty), L_depth (analogous, plus a depth-to-normal coupling loss = arccos(derived_normal · target_normal)), L_normal (Angle Loss measuring directional deviation), L_cam (Huber loss on camera params), L_3dgs (RGB rendering loss + LPIPS perceptual + depth supervision + gradient/consistency). The WorldMirror paper also lists L1 for depth/normals, SmoothL1 for point clouds, Chamfer distance, and cross-entropy for pose classification.
- **Data:** Mix of real and synthetic (https://arxiv.org/html/2510.10726v1 ; https://3d-models.hunyuan.tencent.com/world/world2_0/HY_World_2_0.pdf). ### 6.10 HY-World 1.5 (WorldPlay)
*Tencent Hunyuan (HunyuanWorld team) · 2025-12-17 (release; tech report Dec 2025) · category: Interactive / real-time video-based world model (autoregressive streaming video diffusion)*  
[github.com/Tencent-Hunyuan/HY-WorldPlay](https://github.com/Tencent-Hunyuan/HY-WorldPlay)

- **Definition / role:** HY-World 1.5 is a "systematic framework for interactive world modeling with real-time latency and geometric consistency." Its core is WorldPlay, a streaming video diffusion world model that does real-time (24 FPS), user-interactive world simulation while maintaining long-term geometric consistency. It is the successor to HunyuanWorld 1.0 (which generated offline, non-interactive 3D worlds). Role as a spatial world model: it autoregressively predicts future video chunks (16 frames) conditioned on user keyboard/mouse navigation actions, simulating an explorable 3D environment from a single image or text prompt, supporting first- and third-person perspectives in real and stylized scenes. It is implicit (video-space) rather than an explicit 3D representation, but its outputs are geometrically consistent enough to feed downstream 3D reconstruction (point clouds via WorldMirror), promptable text-triggered events, and infinite world extension. Source: tech report abstract/intro and conclusion, https://3d-models.hunyuan.tencent.com/world/world1_5/HYWorld_1.5_Tech_Report.pdf ; https://arxiv.org/abs/2512.14614 (HY-World 1.5 report); WorldPlay paper https://arxiv.org/abs/... (ref [24], "WorldPlay: Towards Long-Term Geometric Consistency for Real-Time Interactive World Model", Sun et al.).
- **Inputs:** (1) A single image (I2V mode) OR a text prompt describing the world (the conditioning c). (2) User interactive actions via keyboard/mouse: discrete movement commands (W/A/S/D) and view rotation (up/down/left/right). (3) These actions are encoded as a Dual Action Representation: discrete keys AND continuous camera poses (rotation R + translation T), supplied as pose strings or custom JSON trajectory files. (4) During streaming generation, optional text prompts can be injected mid-stream to trigger "promptable events" (weather, lighting, object add/remove, explosions, NPC behavior). The model conditions each next chunk on past observations O(t-1)={x(t-1)...x0}, past actions A(t-1), and current action a(t). Source: tech report Secs 1, 3.2, 4.1, 7.2; GitHub README. Supporting text/vision encoders used (inference pipeline): Qwen2.5-VL-7B-Instruct, byt5-small, Glyph-SDXL-v2, FLUX.1-Redux-dev (per README). Not reported: RGBD or explicit layout inputs.
- **Outputs:** Streaming RGB video generated autoregressively in chunks of 16 frames (= 4 latents per chunk), up to 125 frames default in the released code; tech report demonstrates long-horizon/"infinite" extension and evaluates >=250-frame long-term consistency. Frame rate: 24 FPS (real-time). Resolution: 480P (16:9) per the released models/README and tech report; the WorldPlay arXiv abstract states 720p (resolution figure differs between the released 480P checkpoints and the paper's claim — noted as a discrepancy). Persistence: long-term geometric consistency on revisiting locations via Reconstituted Context Memory (scenes remain coherent when returning along a path). Native output is video/latent, NOT an explicit 3D asset; however generated frames can be fed to external reconstruction (WorldMirror) to produce point clouds / 3D scene representations. No NeRF/mesh/Gaussian-splat is produced directly by the model. Sources: tech report Secs 1, 3.2, 7, 8; GitHub README; arXiv:2512.14614.
- **Structure:** Backbone: a bidirectional video diffusion model built on HunyuanVideo-1.5 (8B-parameter DiT) — a 3D VAE compressing video to a compact latent space plus a Diffusion Transformer (DiT); each DiT block has 3D self-attention, cross-attention (or dual-stream attention) for conditioning, and FFN. A lightweight alternative variant is built on WAN (5B). This bidirectional model is converted into a chunk-wise autoregressive (block-causal attention) model via Diffusion-Forcing-style per-chunk noising (chunks of 4 latents = 16 frames). Key components: (1) Dual Action Representation — discrete keyboard/mouse actions via a zero-init MLP added to the timestep embedding to modulate DiT blocks; continuous camera poses injected into self-attention via Projective Positional Encoding (PRoPE) added through a second attention term (Attn1 + zero_init(Attn2)) on top of 3D RoPE. (2) Reconstituted Context Memory — rebuilds a memory context Ct from past chunks: a temporal memory (L most recent chunks) plus a spatial memory sampled from non-adjacent past frames by geometric-relevance scores (FOV overlap + camera distance); plus Temporal Reframing that re-assigns positional encodings to pull long-past important frames to a small fixed relative distance, countering RoPE long-range decay. (3) WorldCompass RL head/framework (post-training). (4) Context Forcing distillation producing a 4-step student. Inference uses KV-cache, SageAttention, float/matmul quantization, mixed sequence+attention parallelism across 8 GPUs, and NVIDIA Triton streaming with progressive multi-step VAE decoding. Sources: tech report Secs 3-7; GitHub README.
- **Training objectives:** (1) Pre-training: Flow Matching loss L_FM(theta) = E[ || N_theta(z_k,k) - v_k ||^2 ], where z0 is the 3D-VAE video latent, z1~N(0,I), z_k the noisy latent at timestep k, v_k the target velocity (Eq. 1). (2) Autoregressive conversion uses the same flow-matching loss but with different per-chunk noise levels and block-causal attention (Diffusion Forcing style). (3) RL post-training (WorldCompass): policy optimization with complementary reward functions — an action-following score and a visual-quality score for generated content — using a clip-level rollout strategy to mitigate exposure bias; RL algorithm is DiffusionNFT (the GitHub WorldCompass README also references GRPO and uses Hunyuan-WorldMirror / DepthAnythingV3 for camera-pose-based reward). (4) Context Forcing distillation: a distribution matching distillation (DMD) loss, grad_theta L_DMD = E_k[ grad_theta KL(p_theta(x_{0:t}) || p_data(x_{0:t})) ] (Eq. 4), with teacher/student memory contexts aligned (Eq. 5: teacher conditioned on student's memory context minus the self-rolled chunks); the fake-score network is updated via flow matching, the student via the distribution-matching loss (Algorithm 1), enabling 4-step denoising. Sources: tech report Secs 3.1, 3.2, 5.2, 6.1-6.2, Algorithm 1; WorldCompass paper (ref [27]).
- **Data:** A curated dataset of 320K video clips, four categories: (a) 170K clips (53.1%) AAA game recordings, first- and third-person, with directly recorded discrete action signals; (b) 60K clips (18.75%) real-world 3D from the DL3DV dataset, each 3D-reconstructed (3D Gaussian Splatting) with custom camera trajectories designed to simulate interactive navigation; (c) 50K clips (15.625%) synthetic 4D rendered in Unreal Engine with precise ground-truth annotations and controllable lighting; (d) 40K clips (12.5%) real-world natural video from the Sekai dataset with dynamic motion. So it is a mix of real (DL3DV, Sekai), game (AAA), and synthetic (UE) data. Multi-stage filtering: visual-quality assessment (aesthetics scoring, watermark/UI detection, compression-artifact detection), motion-consistency analysis (optical flow, VIPE trajectory validation, erratic-motion removal), dynamic frame-rate augmentation. Annotation: text captions via HunyuanVideo-1.5 caption model; continuous camera poses via VIPE or UE/3D-rendering extraction; discrete action signals derived from camera trajectories (or directly recorded for AAA games). Evaluation set: 600 diverse test cases (real videos, game recordings, AI-generated images). Total hours not reported. Sources: tech report Sec 2 and Fig. 3.
- **Training stages:** Four stages (tech report Fig. 2 and Secs 3-6): (1) Pre-training — train/convert a bidirectional video diffusion model (HunyuanVideo-1.5 backbone) and then into a chunk-wise autoregressive generative model (block-causal, 16-frame chunks) via flow matching + Diffusion Forcing; establishes base interactive generation. (2) Middle-training (Control and Memory Integration) — integrate the Dual Action Representation (discrete keys + continuous PRoPE camera poses) and the Reconstituted Context Memory + Temporal Reframing for action control and long-term geometric consistency. (3) Post-training I — Reinforcement Learning via WorldCompass (clip-level rollout, action-following + visual-quality rewards, DiffusionNFT/GRPO) to improve complex action-following and visual fidelity. (4) Post-training II — Context Forcing distillation, a memory-aware distribution-matching distillation that yields a 4-step autoregressive student for real-time speed while preserving long-range memory and mitigating error accumulation. (Plus a preceding data curation/filtering/annotation pipeline.) Sources: tech report Secs 2-6.
- **Model size:** Backbone: HunyuanVideo-1.5-based ~8B parameters (released "WorldPlay-8B"); lightweight alternative built on WAN ~5B ("WorldPlay-5B"). Released checkpoints: Bidirectional-480P-I2V, Autoregressive-480P-I2V, Autoregressive-480P-I2V-RL, Autoregressive-480P-I2V-distill (4-step). The tech report does not give a precise total parameter count beyond the 8B/5B backbone sizes; auxiliary encoders (Qwen2.5-VL-7B, byt5-small, Glyph-SDXL-v2, FLUX.1-Redux-dev) are separate inference components. Exact full-model parameter count: not reported (n/r) beyond backbone sizes. Sources: GitHub README (model variants & 8B/5B backbones).
- **Training compute:** Not reported. The tech report does not state the training GPU type, count, or GPU-hours/days for pretraining. Disclosed compute figures are inference/training memory and parallelism only: inference uses sequence+attention parallelism across 8 GPUs (AR distilled 125-frame inference ~28GB at sp=8, ~34GB at sp=4, ~72GB at sp=1; training 125 frames ~60GB at sp=8 per README). The WorldCompass RL stage (GitHub worldcompass README) requires a minimum of 8 GPUs with ~96GB memory each, multi-node recommended, with FlashAttention 2.7.3. Overall end-to-end training compute (total GPU count/hours): n/r. Sources: GitHub README; worldcompass/README.md.
- **Key results:** Reconstruction-based quantitative results (tech report Table 2) vs baselines (CameraCtrl, SEVA, ViewCrafter, Gen3C, VMem, Matrix-Game-2.0, GameCraft). Short-term (61 frames): PSNR 21.92 (best), SSIM 0.702, LPIPS 0.247, Rdist 0.031, Tdist 0.121. Long-term (>=250 frames): PSNR 18.94 (best), SSIM 0.585, LPIPS 0.371, Rdist 0.332 (best long-term geometric consistency), Tdist 0.797. The "w/o Context Forcing" variant is worse long-term (PSNR 16.27, Rdist 0.611), showing Context Forcing's value. It is the only real-time method among memory-consistent baselines and the only one with continuous+discrete action space plus real-time + long-term consistency + general domain (Table 1). VBench: leads on aesthetic/imaging quality, consistency, motion smoothness. Human preference: strongly preferred over GameCraft (88.5% vs 11.5%), ViewCrafter (92.1% vs 7.9%), Matrix-Game-2.0 (78.4% vs 21.6%), Gen3C (72.9% vs 27.1%); near-parity with its own bidirectional teacher (48.1% vs 51.9%). Runs at 24 FPS with 4-step denoising. First open-source model claiming both real-time (24 FPS) generation and long-term geometric consistency. Applications: 3D reconstruction (point clouds via WorldMirror), promptable text-triggered events, video continuation, infinite world extension. Sources: tech report Sec 8, Tables 1-2, Fig. 9.

### 6.11 WonderWorld
*Stanford University and MIT (Hong-Xing "Koven" Yu, Haoyi Duan, Charles Herrmann, William T. Freeman, Jiajun Wu) · 2024-06-13 (arXiv v1); published CVPR 2025 (Highlight) · category: Spatial / 3D world generation (interactive single-image-to-3D-scene generation)*  
[kovenyu.com/wonderworld](https://kovenyu.com/wonderworld/)

- **Definition / role:** WonderWorld is an interactive 3D scene-generation framework that takes a single image plus text prompts and generates connected, diverse, explorable 3D scenes in under ~10 seconds per scene on a single GPU. As a spatial world model it functions as a generator/extender of persistent 3D worlds: the user moves a virtual camera, points to where a new scene should appear and gives a text description of its content, and the system synthesizes new 3D geometry that is geometrically connected to the already-built scene, letting the user interactively "wander" through and grow an unbounded virtual world. It is not a dynamics/action-prediction model; it is a 3D spatial scene generator emphasizing low-latency interactivity (the core contribution is speed, vs. minutes-per-scene prior work). Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.09394 , https://kovenyu.com/wonderworld/
- **Inputs:** A single RGB input image depicting the starting scene, plus user-provided text prompts that specify the content of new scenes and a user-specified virtual camera placement/location indicating where the next scene should be generated. Internally it derives monocular depth and surface normals from the input image (not user-provided). It does NOT require multiple input views, video, ground-truth camera pose, or action sequences. Source: https://arxiv.org/html/2406.09394v3
- **Outputs:** A 3D scene represented as FLAGS (Fast Layered Gaussian Surfels) — i.e., 2D Gaussian surfels (flat/thin Gaussians, z-scale shrunk to epsilon for surface representation) organized into three layers: foreground, background, and sky. These render in real time via Gaussian splatting to produce free-viewpoint RGB images as the user moves the camera. Output worlds are persistent and connected: each newly generated scene is geometrically attached to the existing accumulated world, and scenes are kept geometrically consistent across boundaries via "guided depth diffusion." Generation latency is <10 s per scene (reported ~9.5 s). Explicit output image resolution and a numeric rendering FPS are not reported (real-time rendering is inherited from the Gaussian-splatting representation). Source: https://arxiv.org/html/2406.09394v3 , https://kovenyu.com/wonderworld/
- **Structure:** Training-free pipeline assembling pretrained off-the-shelf models around the novel FLAGS 3D representation. Components: (1) FLAGS — Fast Layered Gaussian Surfels: each surfel has 3D position p, orientation quaternion q, 2D scales s=[s_x,s_y], opacity o, and view-independent RGB color c; covariance built from scales+quaternion with z-axis collapsed to epsilon (flat surfels); scene decomposed into foreground / background / sky layers. (2) Stable Diffusion Inpainting for outpainting and per-layer (e.g., occluded-region) inpainting. (3) Marigold (latent diffusion) for monocular depth estimation and a Marigold-style model for surface-normal estimation. (4) OneFormer for semantic segmentation (sky and foreground objects). (5) GPT-4 / GPT-4V (LLM/VLM) for generating scene descriptions and prompts. (6) CLIP used for evaluation/consistency metrics. (7) Geometry-based initialization (closed-form, derived from a Nyquist-sampling argument) that places surfels from depth+normal so optimization converges fast. (8) "Guided depth diffusion" — gradient guidance injected into the Marigold depth-diffusion sampling so that the estimated depth of a newly generated region matches (partial conditioning on) the existing scene's depth where they overlap, enforcing cross-scene geometric consistency. No diffusion-transformer/video backbone is trained; there is no learned tokenizer or trained world-model network. Source: https://arxiv.org/html/2406.09394v3
- **Training objectives:** No network training objective — WonderWorld trains no neural networks. The only optimization is a fast per-layer fitting of the FLAGS surfels to images. Photometric reconstruction loss per layer = 0.8·L1 + 0.2·L_D-SSIM (i.e., a weighted L1 + D-SSIM image loss, the standard 3D-Gaussian-splatting reconstruction objective), optimized ~100 Adam iterations per layer. The guided depth diffusion adds a guidance gradient term of the form ∇_{d_t} || D_{t-1} ⊙ M_guide − D_guide ⊙ M_guide ||^2 (squared error between the diffusing depth and the guidance depth on a guidance mask) injected into the depth-diffusion sampling, rather than a trained loss. Source: https://arxiv.org/html/2406.09394v3
- **Data:** No training dataset; the method is training-free and relies entirely on pretrained models. Evaluation uses public real/synthetic test images from prior work plus newly collected examples. Reported evaluation set: 28 test scenes (7 scenes x 4 test examples: city, campus, nature, fantasy). Human study: 204 two-alternative-forced-choice (2AFC) preference judgments on bird's-eye-view renderings (98.0-98.6% preference for WonderWorld). Novel-view consistency assessed over 9 sudoku-like novel views per scene. Single-image 3D system; no hours/clips of video. Source: https://arxiv.org/html/2406.09394v3
- **Training stages:** Zero training stages (no pretrain / fine-tune / post-train / distillation / RLHF). All learned weights come from frozen pretrained models (Stable Diffusion Inpainting, Marigold depth/normal, OneFormer, GPT-4/GPT-4V, CLIP). The per-scene "pipeline" stages are inference-time only: (a) LLM scene description/prompting, (b) outpainting (~2.1 s), (c) layer generation/inpainting (~2.3 s), (d) depth estimation with guided depth diffusion (~2.5 s), (e) normal estimation (~0.8 s), (f) geometry-based FLAGS initialization + ~100-iteration surfel optimization (~1.9 s) — totaling ~9.5 s per scene. Source: https://arxiv.org/html/2406.09394v3
- **Model size:** Not reported as a single trained-parameter count (WonderWorld trains no model of its own; the core pipeline is training-free). Total parameters depend on frozen pretrained components used (Stable Diffusion Inpaint, Marigold depth/normal, OneFormer, GPT-4/GPT-4V, CLIP); the paper reports no aggregate parameter count. n/r. Source: https://arxiv.org/html/2406.09394v3
- **Training compute:** No training compute (training-free). Inference/generation: a single NVIDIA RTX A6000 GPU, generating a 3D scene in under 10 seconds (Table 1 reports 9.5 s per scene). GPU-hours/days not applicable since no training is performed. Note: paper writes 'A6000' (= NVIDIA RTX A6000). Sources: https://arxiv.org/abs/2406.09394 , https://arxiv.org/html/2406.09394v3 , https://kovenyu.com/wonderworld/
- **Key results:** Generates a connected 3D scene in <10 s (~9.5 s) on a single A6000 GPU — roughly 80–100x faster than prior single-image/text 3D-scene methods (WonderJourney, LucidDreamer, Text2Room reported at ~749.5–798.1 s per scene). Novel FLAGS representation + geometry-based initialization removes the need for multiple-view generation and slow per-scene geometry optimization. Guided depth diffusion enforces geometric consistency where new scenes connect to existing ones. Evaluated with CLIP score, CLIP consistency, CLIP-IQA+, Q-Align, CLIP aesthetic score, and a human 2AFC study (204 judgments) showing strong preference; enables interactive "wandering" through an unbounded, persistent virtual world. CVPR 2025 Highlight. Source: https://arxiv.org/html/2406.09394v3 , https://kovenyu.com/wonderworld/

### 6.12 WonderJourney: Going from Anywhere to Everywhere
*Stanford University and Google (Google Research / Google DeepMind contributors). Authors: Hong-Xing (Koven) Yu, Haoyi Duan, Junhwa Hur, Kyle Sargent, Michael Rubinstein, William T. Freeman, Forrester Cole, Deqing Sun, Noah Snavely, Jiajun Wu, Charles Herrmann. (https://kovenyu.com/wonderjourney/) · arXiv v1 submitted 2023-12-06; v2 revised 2024-04-12. Published at CVPR 2024. (https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.03884) · category: Perpetual 3D scene generation / "wonderjourney" generation — a modular, training-free pipeline (LLM + text-driven point-cloud 3D generation + VLM). Spatial world model in the sense of an explicit 3D-geometry-grounded scene generator, not a learned action-conditioned dynamics model. (https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.03884)*  
[arxiv.org/abs/2312.03884](https://arxiv.org/abs/2312.03884)

- **Definition / role:** WonderJourney is a modular framework for "perpetual 3D scene generation": from a single user-provided starting point it auto-regressively generates a long sequence of diverse yet coherently connected 3D scenes ("a wonderjourney") that a camera flies through. Its role as a (spatial) world model is geometric and explicit rather than learned-dynamics: it maintains a persistent 3D point-cloud representation of the world, places new cameras along a trajectory, renders partial views from that geometry, and out-paints/in-paints to extend the world — so the "world state" is an accumulating 3D point cloud kept geometrically consistent across scenes via depth re-registration and occlusion handling. It is NOT an action-conditioned video/dynamics world model and learns no dynamics; it is a controllable spatial scene generator. The paper explicitly states the framework "does not require any training." (https://arxiv.org/html/2312.03884v2, https://kovenyu.com/wonderjourney/)

No actions, no explicit camera pose input from the user, no RGBD or layout input — camera trajectories/poses are chosen internally (a new camera C_{i+1} is placed at an appropriate distance from C_i). (https://arxiv.org/html/2312.03884v2, https://kovenyu.com/wonderjourney/, https://github.com/KovenYu/WonderJourney)
- **Outputs:** A sequence of connected 3D scenes represented as colored 3D point clouds, rendered along camera trajectories into RGB frames and compiled into an output MP4 video (configurable FPS via save_fps). Per-scene the explicit representation is a point cloud (built from estimated depth); there is no mesh, NeRF, or 3D Gaussian splat. Image/render resolution is 512x512. Persistence: an accumulating, geometrically consistent point-cloud world maintained across many scenes (demos up to ~30 generated scenes). No fixed FPS is reported (configurable). (https://arxiv.org/html/2312.03884v2, https://github.com/KovenYu/WonderJourney)
- **Structure:** Modular three-component, training-free pipeline. (1) Scene-description module: GPT-4 (LLM) auto-regressively generates structured scene info from past scenes — scene name, three primary entities (nouns/adjectives via lexical filtering), a ~50-word background description, and a consistent style. (2) Text-driven point-cloud 3D scene generation: estimate per-pixel depth with MiDaS v3.1 (DPT, dpt_beit_large_512 / DPT-ViT backbone) to lift the image to a colored 3D point cloud; SAM + OneFormer provide pixel-grouping segments and sky segmentation; depth boundary refinement replaces depth with the median within a segment when its disparity range is low; OneFormer-segmented sky is assigned a high depth value with a far-plane clip (F = 0.0015); to advance, a new camera is placed, the existing point cloud is rendered to a partial image, and a Stable Diffusion 2 inpainting model out-paints the missing regions with text guidance, then the new content is lifted to 3D and merged. (3) VLM visual validation: GPT-4V inspects each generated scene via textual queries for artifacts (photo borders, painting frames, out-of-focus objects) and triggers regeneration. Rendering uses PyTorch3D; spaCy (en_core_web_sm) for NLP parsing. (https://arxiv.org/html/2312.03884v2, https://github.com/KovenYu/WonderJourney)

Plus a geometric "occlusion handling by re-rendering consistency" step that re-renders the new scene, detects inconsistent/disoccluded pixels, and corrects their depth. There is no diffusion training loss, GAN loss, or RLHF. (https://arxiv.org/html/2312.03884v2)

### 6.13 WonderTurbo
*GigaAI; Peking University; Institute of Automation, Chinese Academy of Sciences; Zhejiang University · 2025-04-03 (arXiv v1); accepted to ICCV 2025 · category: Interactive 3D scene/world generation (single-image, text-controllable, real-time spatial world model)*  
[arxiv.org/abs/2504.02261](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.02261)

- **Definition / role:** WonderTurbo is described by its authors as "the first real-time interactive 3D scene generation framework capable of generating novel perspectives of 3D scenes within 0.72 seconds." As a spatial world model, it lets a user start from a single image and then freely move a real-time rendering camera and supply text prompts to interactively create and extend a coherent, persistent, explorable 3D world. Its role is online/interactive (not offline fixed-scene): at each user step it generates new appearance + geometry for the requested viewpoint and incrementally fuses it into a maintained global 3D representation, accelerating both geometry and appearance modeling to reach real-time speed (claimed 15x speedup vs prior online methods like WonderWorld which needs ~10s). Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.02261 ; https://wonderturbo.github.io ; https://openaccess.thecvf.com/content/ICCV2025/papers/Ni_WonderTurbo_Generating_Interactive_3D_World_in_0.72_Seconds_ICCV_2025_paper.pdf
- **Inputs:** Single input RGB image (scene seed) plus user-provided text/textual description specifying content of new scenes, plus a user-specified viewpoint/camera pose (the user moves a real-time rendering camera; supports panoramic camera paths and casual walking trajectories). Internally at iteration i the modules consume: the rendered RGB image I_render^i and rendered depth map D_render^i produced from the current 3D scene, and camera pose P^i. So effective input modalities = image + text + camera pose/trajectory + (internally) rendered depth. No actions/layout/RGBD sensor input from the user is required. Sources: paper Sec 3.1, Fig 2 — https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.02261
- **Outputs:** A persistent, globally consistent 3D scene represented as 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS): the system maintains a global Gaussian representation G_global that is incrementally fused/expanded each interaction (G_global^{i+1} <- G_global^i ∪ G_local^i \ C). From this 3DGS, novel-view RGB images are rendered in real time (and depth maps are rendered/used). Intermediate outputs: FastPaint produces new-view appearance image I_target^i; QuickDepth produces a completed depth map D_target^i; StepSplat produces local Gaussians G_local^i. Persistence: yes — the global Gaussian model is retained and updated across viewpoints, ensuring continuous/consistent 3D representation. Timing/"FPS": one new-perspective generation = 0.72s total (FastPaint 0.22s + QuickDepth 0.24s + StepSplat 0.26s; Table 1 reports geometry 0.50s + appearance 0.22s), i.e. roughly ~1.4 interactions/sec, on an H20 GPU. Output image resolution and explicit FPS number: not reported. Mesh/NeRF: not used (representation is 3DGS). Sources: paper Sec 3.1, Eq 9, Table 1 — https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.02261
- **Structure:** Three accelerated modules feeding a 3DGS pipeline. (1) StepSplat (geometry): a feed-forward 3DGS predictor extended to interactive/incremental generation. Backbone = RepVGG (cited ref [13]) extracting image features F_e and matching features F_m. A "Feature Memory" module stores (pose, matching-feature) tuples of previous views. For the current view it selects N_v nearest stored poses, builds a Depth-Guided Cost Volume via plane-sweep stereo warping (N_d depth candidates sampled around QuickDepth depth), and refines/upsamples the cost volume with an additional 2D U-Net (ref [38]). Depth is predicted via softmax over the cost volume; depth values are unprojected as 3DGS centers; remaining Gaussian params (opacity/alpha, covariance/Sigma, color c) decoded similar to MVSplat. An Incremental Fusion step prunes redundant/conflicting global Gaussians (depth-consistency constraint) and merges new local Gaussians. (2) QuickDepth (depth completion): lightweight depth-completion model initialized from a light pre-trained depth estimator (ZoeDepth, ref [3]); inputs = target-frame RGB + incomplete/warped depth map + binary validity mask; outputs completed dense depth. (3) FastPaint (appearance): a 2-step diffusion inpainting model built by distilling and fine-tuning a pretrained latent diffusion / Stable Diffusion inpainting model (Rombach et al. LDM, ref [37]), using ODE trajectory preservation + reformulation from Hyper-SD (ref [36]). Representation: 3D Gaussian Splatting throughout. Sources: paper Sec 3.2-3.4, Fig 2-3 — https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.02261
- **Training objectives:** Per-module objectives reported (no single global loss). (1) StepSplat: trained with an RGB rendering reconstruction/photometric supervision — image sequences are fed in one-by-one to build the global Gaussian representation, which is rendered from novel viewpoints with the rendered RGB images serving as supervision (paper says "RGB images serving as supervision"; specific photometric/perceptual loss terms such as L1/SSIM/LPIPS are not explicitly named). (2) QuickDepth: supervised by the target frame's ground-truth depth via an L1 loss. (3) FastPaint: trained via knowledge distillation (step reduction) from the pretrained diffusion model, leveraging ODE-trajectory preservation/reformulation (Hyper-SD-style) plus fine-tuning on the constructed interactive-inpainting dataset; the exact distillation loss formulation is not written out. Sources: paper Sec 3.2 (Training of StepSplat), Sec 3.3 (L1 loss), Sec 3.4 — https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.02261

For QuickDepth/FastPaint, masks obtained by projecting depth between adjacent frames (warped depth-mask pairs / binary validity masks) — confirmed.

Effectively: (a) StepSplat trained feed-forward with novel-view RGB rendering supervision; (b) QuickDepth initialized from a pretrained depth model (ZoeDepth) then trained with L1 depth supervision on warped/masked depth inputs; (c) FastPaint produced by distilling a pretrained Stable Diffusion/LDM inpainting model down to 2 inference steps (ODE-trajectory preservation/reformulation, Hyper-SD style) plus fine-tuning on the interactive-inpainting dataset. So conceptually a pretrain-then-distill/fine-tune scheme for the diffusion module and from-scratch/init-then-train for the geometry and depth modules. No RLHF. ### 6.14 WonderVerse
*Academic — The Chinese University of Hong Kong (CUHK) and collaborators (authors: Hao Feng, Zhi Zuo, Jia-Hui Pan, Ka-Hei Hui, Qi Dou, Jingyu Hu, Zhengzhe Liu). Accepted at CVM 2026. · First arXiv submission 2025-03-12 (v1); latest revision v5. Title: "WonderVerse: Extendable 3D Scene Generation with Video Generative Models" (https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.09160). · category: Academic (arXiv) — 3D scene generation / spatial world model; training-free pipeline built on video generative foundation models.*  
[arxiv.org/abs/2503.09160](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.09160)

- **Definition / role:** WonderVerse is a "simple but effective framework for generating extendable 3D scenes" that "leverages the powerful world-level priors embedded within video generative foundation models to create highly immersive and geometrically coherent 3D environments" (abstract, https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.09160). As a spatial world model, its role is to convert the implicit world-level priors learned by pretrained video generators into explicit, navigable, geometrically consistent, large-scale 3D scenes that can be iteratively extended. Unlike prior iterative depth-estimation + image-inpainting pipelines (which cause geometric distortion), it uses video generation as the core scene-synthesis engine and adds correction/enhancement modules to enforce 3D consistency. The authors emphasize their contribution "lies not in proposing new foundation models or reconstruction algorithms, but in demonstrating how recent video generative priors can be reliably harnessed for extendable 3D scene generation" (https://arxiv.org/html/2503.09160).
- **Inputs:** Text-only primary input: "Given a text prompt P, we use text-guided video generation model ... to obtain an initial video" (https://arxiv.org/html/2503.09160). Camera trajectories for controllable scene extension are generated/controlled internally (LLM-driven, via GPT-4o mini) rather than supplied by the user. No image, RGBD, action, or user-provided pose input is required as a core modality in the reported pipeline (camera control is exercised through the extension module / Gen-3 Alpha's camera control capability). Multimodal image conditioning is not reported as a primary input.
- **Outputs:** Explicit 3D scene representations: primarily 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS); also compatible with point clouds and mesh, since the framework "is compatible with various 3D reconstruction methods" (abstract). Intermediate output is generated video, denoted V_init ∈ R^(T×H×W×3). Exact output resolution, frame count (T,H,W given only as symbolic variables), FPS, and persistence/memory metrics are not reported numerically in the paper. Final deliverable: an extendable, navigable, geometrically coherent large-scale 3D environment renderable from novel viewpoints.
- **Structure:** Training-free pipeline composed of pretrained off-the-shelf components: (1) Video generation backbone — "Hunyuan video model" for initial scenes and "Gen-3 Alpha" for extensions ("due to its superior camera control capability"). (2) 3D reconstruction — 3D Gaussian Splatting (primary); DUSt3R demonstrated for compatibility; COLMAP (Structure-from-Motion) for camera pose estimation. (3) Video Geometric Correction (abnormal sequence detection) module — filters geometrically inconsistent videos by analyzing camera-trajectory smoothness from COLMAP poses, using discontinuity thresholds (reported T1=5, T2=0.5). (4) 3D Geometric Enhancement module — depth via Depth Anything V2 and surface normals via a Dense Prediction Transformer (DPT) with a hybrid backbone, aligned to the 3D scene via linear regression to enforce depth/normal consistency. (5) Controllable 3D scene extension — LLM-driven (GPT-4o mini) semantic scene expansion with camera-trajectory generation. No new neural network is trained; it is an orchestration/optimization framework over frozen foundation models.
- **Training objectives:** No network training; the only objective is a per-scene 3DGS reconstruction/refinement optimization loss (Eq. 5): L = L_C + λ_d·L_D + λ_n·L_N, combining a photometric/color loss (L_C), a depth loss (log-depth variant, L_D), and a normal-consistency loss (L_N) with smoothness regularization (https://arxiv.org/html/2503.09160). These enforce appearance fidelity plus geometric (depth/normal) consistency between the generated video and the reconstructed 3D scene. No cross-entropy/diffusion training loss, no RLHF, no distillation loss is reported.
- **Data:** No training datasets are used — the method is training-free and relies on pretrained foundation models (Hunyuan Video, Gen-3 Alpha, Depth Anything V2, DPT, GPT-4o mini, COLMAP, 3DGS). The paper reports no hours/clips/scenes of training data and no real-vs-synthetic training corpus, because nothing is trained. Evaluation is on generated indoor and outdoor scenes (qualitative + quantitative metrics), not on a named benchmark dataset of fixed scale. (https://arxiv.org/html/2503.09160)
- **Training stages:** Zero training stages — explicitly training-free / zero-shot: it "uses pre-trained models ... without mentioning fine-tuning" and the contribution is harnessing existing video priors, not training models (https://arxiv.org/html/2503.09160). The runtime pipeline instead has inference stages: (1) text-to-video initial scene generation; (2) abnormal/video geometric correction (camera-trajectory filtering); (3) 3D reconstruction (3DGS) with geometric-enhancement optimization (depth/normal); (4) LLM-driven controllable scene extension and re-reconstruction, iterated to enlarge the scene. These are inference stages, not training/pretrain/fine-tune/RLHF stages.
- **Model size:** n/r (not reported). The paper states no parameter counts for the overall framework; component model sizes (Hunyuan Video, Gen-3 Alpha, Depth Anything V2, DPT, GPT-4o mini) are not specified in the paper. Since the framework trains no new model, it has no native parameter count of its own.
- **Training compute:** n/r (not reported). The paper provides no GPU/TPU type or count, no training time, and no inference latency or hardware specifications. (Gen-3 Alpha is accessed as a hosted/commercial model; the GitHub repo https://github.com/FH2070/WonderVerse is CUDA-heavy (~89% CUDA) for the depth-accumulating Gaussian rasterizer but lists no compute requirements; "Complete code will be released soon".)
- **Key results:** Outperforms extendable-3D-scene baselines (LucidDreamer; WonderWorld) on its reported metrics (Table 1, https://arxiv.org/html/2503.09160). Outdoor: CLIP-Score 0.9219 (vs WonderWorld 0.7817), Q-Align 3.2229 (vs 2.8302), NIQE 0.7806 (vs 0.7127). Indoor: CLIP-Score 0.9639 (vs LucidDreamer 0.4633), Q-Align 4.2680 (vs 3.9870). Comparison "focuses on extendable 3D scene generation pipelines that share the same task setting of iterative scene expansion." Authors claim an "elegant and simple pipeline" that delivers extendable, highly-realistic 3D scenes, "markedly outperforming existing works that rely on more complex architectures." Qualitative: avoids geometric distortion typical of depth-estimation+inpainting methods; supports controllable large-scale extension and multiple reconstruction backends (3DGS/DUSt3R).

### 6.15 DimensionX
*HKUST, Tsinghua University, Nanyang Technological University (authors: Wenqiang Sun, Shuo Chen, Fangfu Liu, Zilong Chen, Yueqi Duan, Jun Zhang, Yikai Wang). Accepted to ICCV 2025. · 2024-11-07 (arXiv v1) · category: Generative spatial/4D world model — controllable video diffusion for 3D and 4D scene generation from a single image.*  
[arxiv.org/abs/2411.04928](https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.04928)

- **Definition / role:** DimensionX is a framework that creates photorealistic 3D and 4D scenes from a single image using controllable video diffusion. Its role as a (spatial) world model: it treats the spatial structure of a 3D scene and the temporal evolution of a 4D scene as decoupled axes that can both be expressed through sequences of video frames. By generating consistent video that sweeps the camera through a scene (spatial axis) and/or advances object/scene dynamics (temporal axis), it produces video that is then lifted into explicit 3D/4D geometry. The core insight is that a video diffusion model, made controllable along separate spatial and temporal dimensions via "ST-Director," can serve as a generative engine for reconstructing navigable 3D and dynamic 4D worlds from monocular input (https://arxiv.org/abs/2411.04928, https://arxiv.org/html/2411.04928v1, https://chenshuo20.github.io/DimensionX/).
- **Inputs:** Primary input is a single RGB image (for 3D and 4D generation). Sparse multi-view RGB images are also supported as input for sparse-view 3D scene generation. Camera trajectory / viewpoint control is supplied implicitly by selecting which spatial S-Director LoRA(s) to apply (the released set covers 6 degrees of freedom bidirectionally plus orbital up/down/left/right), rather than via explicit numeric pose conditioning. No text prompt, depth (RGBD), explicit action vectors, or scene-layout inputs are required (https://arxiv.org/html/2411.04928v1, https://chenshuo20.github.io/DimensionX/, https://github.com/wenqsun/DimensionX). "Not reported" for any explicit camera-pose numeric input format.
- **Outputs:** Intermediate outputs are controllable RGB video frames: standard inference produces 48-frame / 6-second clips; an extended backbone produces 145-frame (~18-second) videos including 360-degree orbit sweeps. Generation resolution is 480x320; for 4D it generates up to 32 multi-view videos. Final outputs are explicit 3D and 4D scene representations: 3D scenes as 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS, optimized 7,000 steps from 49 video frames, DUSt3R point-cloud initialization); 4D scenes as deformable 3D Gaussian Splatting optimized on 32-view multi-view videos. FPS / persistence beyond clip length: not reported. Released artifacts also include the generated videos themselves (https://arxiv.org/html/2411.04928v1, https://github.com/wenqsun/DimensionX).
- **Structure:** Base video diffusion model: CogVideoX-5B-I2V, a DiT (Diffusion Transformer) image-to-video model operating in a 3D-VAE latent space (the repo notes CogVideoX1.5/HunyuanVideo/WanX could also serve as backbone). Key component "ST-Director" decouples spatial vs temporal control via two sets of dimension-aware LoRAs (rank 256) applied to the attention layers: S-Director (spatial, camera-viewpoint variation with time frozen) and T-Director (temporal, object motion with camera frozen). The backbone is extended from 49 to 145 frames by modifying RoPE positional embeddings. Additional components: a trajectory-aware mechanism / adaptive S-Director selection plus a video-interpolation model (dual first/last-frame conditioning) for sparse-view 3D; an identity-preserving denoising strategy for 4D (reference-latent sharing z_t = lambda*z_t + (1-lambda)*z_ref,t in early steps, plus SDEdit-style appearance refinement). Downstream reconstruction uses DUSt3R for point-cloud init, confidence-aware 3DGS for 3D, and deformable 3DGS for 4D (https://arxiv.org/html/2411.04928v1, https://github.com/wenqsun/DimensionX).
- **Training objectives:** L_diffusion = E[ || epsilon - epsilon_theta(z_t, t, z1, z2, c) ||_2^2 ] (noise-prediction diffusion loss for the LoRA/interpolation video model); 3D 3DGS loss L_conf = C*(0.8*L1 + 0.2*L_ssim + 0.3*L_lpips) with confidence map C; 4D deformable-GS loss L = L1 + L_tv + L_ssim (https://arxiv.org/html/2411.04928v1).
- **Data:** Backbone (CogVideoX) is pretrained on web-scale video by its original authors; DimensionX's own LoRA/fine-tuning data: S-Director trained on spatial-variant clips curated from DL3DV-10K (multi-view 3D scene dataset), ~100 videos per trajectory type, with 13 S-Director types (12 DoF + orbital). T-Director trained on ~100 temporal-variant clips from OpenVid (filtered via optical flow for camera-stationary motion). Extended-length backbone and interpolation model fine-tuned on high-quality >145-frame videos from RealEstate-10K and OpenVid. A custom DimensionX orbit-video training set is released. Data is a mix of real-world (RealEstate-10K, OpenVid) and real captured multi-view (DL3DV-10K) scenes; explicit total hours not reported. Evaluation benchmarks: Tanks-and-Temples, MipNeRF360, LLFF/NeRF-LLFF, DL3DV-10K (3D), and Neu3D (4D) (https://arxiv.org/html/2411.04928v1).
- **Training stages:** Four-ish stages, all fine-tuning on top of frozen CogVideoX-5B: (1) Backbone long-video fine-tune — full fine-tune to extend to 145 frames (~2,000 steps, LR 5e-5). (2) ST-Director LoRA training — train S-Director and T-Director LoRAs separately (3,000 steps each, LR 1e-3, rank 256) on dimension-variant data. (3) Video-interpolation model training — dual first/last-frame conditioning for sparse-view 3D (2,000 steps, LR 5e-5). (4) Adaptive S-Director / early-stopping interpolation-guidance training (~1,000 steps). No pretraining-from-scratch, no RLHF, no distillation stage. Downstream per-scene optimization (3DGS 7,000 steps; deformable 4D-GS) is reconstruction, not model training (https://arxiv.org/html/2411.04928v1).
- **Model size:** Base model CogVideoX-5B-I2V => approximately 5 billion parameters. The added ST-Director modules are LoRA adapters of rank 256 (lightweight; exact added parameter count not reported). No other parameter counts reported (https://github.com/wenqsun/DimensionX, https://arxiv.org/html/2411.04928v1).
- **Training compute:** Training GPU type, count, and wall-clock time are not reported in the paper. Only inference compute is documented in the repo: ~26.33 GB VRAM for 48-frame/6s generation (~3 min on NVIDIA A100/A800), and ~30.52 GB for 145-frame/18s 360-degree orbit (~6 min on NVIDIA A6000). Training compute: not reported (https://github.com/wenqsun/DimensionX, https://arxiv.org/html/2411.04928v1).
- **Key results:** Controllable video generation (vs CogVideoX / Dream Machine 1.6): Consistency 97.69, Dynamic 47.06, Aesthetic 70.82 — best on all three. 3D single-view on DL3DV-10K: PSNR 18.28 (vs ZeroNVS 12.39, ViewCrafter 14.78), SSIM 0.642, LPIPS 0.215. 3D sparse-view on DL3DV-10K: PSNR 21.69 (vs InstantSplat 18.30, ViewCrafter 19.19), SSIM 0.780, LPIPS 0.124. 4D results on Neu3D are largely qualitative; 4D quantitative metrics not reported. Demonstrates single-image -> navigable 3DGS scenes and dynamic 4D scenes, plus 360-degree orbit generation. Accepted to ICCV 2025; Apache-2.0; released 12 DoF + 4 orbit camera LoRAs, a T-Director checkpoint, and a 360-orbit checkpoint on HuggingFace (https://arxiv.org/html/2411.04928v1, https://github.com/wenqsun/DimensionX).
- **Verification (size/compute/data), confidence high:** Verified against both primary sources: arxiv.org/html/2411.04928v1 (paper) and github.com/wenqsun/DimensionX (repo).

SIZE: Confirmed. Base model is CogVideoX-5B-I2V (repo: "CogVideoX-5B-I2V-sat checkpoint"); the "5B" in the official model name corresponds to ~5 billion parameters. ST-Director modules are LoRA adapters with rank 256 (paper: "we set the LoRA rank to 256"). No explicit added-parameter count is stated in either source. Repo describes 12 fundamental camera LoRA modules + 4 orbit-style LoRA modules + separate T-Director. Claim accurate.

COMPUTE: Confirmed. Training GPU type/count/wall-clock time are NOT reported. Paper reports training hyperparameters only (3000 steps @ lr 1e-3 for ST-Director LoRA; interpolation 2000 steps @ 5e-5 + 1000 steps) but no hardware/time. Only inference compute documented in repo: ~26.33 GB VRAM, ~3 min for 48-frame/6s on NVIDIA A100/A800; ~30.52 GB VRAM, ~6 min for 145-frame/18s 360-orbit on NVIDIA A6000. Both figures match exactly. Marking training compute "not reported" is correct.

DATA: Confirmed. S-Director: ~100 rendered videos per trajectory ("For each S-Director type, 100 videos are rendered") curated from DL3DV-10K; 13 S-Director types = 12 DoF (6 DoF x pos/neg) + 1 orbital, exactly as paper states. T-Director: ~100 temporal-variant clips from OpenVid, filtered via optical flow for camera-stationary motion (camera stationary C(t)=C0). Extended-length backbone/interpolation fine-tuned on >145-frame videos filtered from RealEstate-10K and OpenVid. Custom orbit-video training set released (huggingface.co/datasets/wenqsun/DimensionX_Video). Evaluation benchmarks Tanks-and-Temples, MipNeRF360, LLFF/NeRF-LLFF, DL3DV-10K (3D), Neu3D (4D) confirmed verbatim. Total training hours not reported — correct.

### 6.16 CAT3D: Create Anything in 3D with Multi-View Diffusion Models
*Google DeepMind / Google Research (collaboration with Columbia University) · 2024-05-16 (arXiv v1); published NeurIPS 2024 · category: Image/few-view-to-3D generative world model (multi-view diffusion + 3D reconstruction)*  
[arxiv.org/abs/2405.10314](https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.10314)

- **Definition / role:** CAT3D is a method for creating 3D content (objects and full scenes) from any number of input images — including a single image, few images, or even a text prompt (via a text-to-image model upstream). It works by simulating the real-world multi-view capture process with a multi-view diffusion model: given input view(s) and a set of desired target camera poses, it generates a large set of consistent novel views, which are then fed to a robust 3D reconstruction pipeline to produce a real-time-renderable 3D representation. As a spatial world model, its role is to "hallucinate" geometrically and photometrically consistent observations of a 3D scene from sparse evidence, decoupling generation (the diffusion prior over what the scene looks like from unseen viewpoints) from reconstruction (distilling those views into a persistent, explorable 3D model). It is the successor to ReconFusion and decouples generation from reconstruction for an order-of-magnitude speedup. Source: https://arxiv.org/abs/2405.10314 , https://cat3d.github.io/
- **Inputs:** Modalities: (a) one or more RGB images (1, 3, 6, or 9 conditional input views are evaluated), and (b) target camera poses for the views to be generated. Camera pose is supplied to the model as a "raymap" — a per-pixel camera ray representation (ray origins and directions) matching the latent's spatial dimensions, used in place of the time embedding of a video diffusion model. For single-image input, the camera intrinsics/scene scale are estimated (a default field of view is assumed when unknown). Text is not a direct input to CAT3D itself, but a text-to-image model can produce the single image that seeds the pipeline. No depth (RGBD), no actions, no explicit layout input. Source: https://arxiv.org/html/2405.10314v1
- **Outputs:** Two stages of output: (1) the multi-view diffusion model outputs synthesized novel-view RGB images at 512x512 resolution (decoded from a 64x64x8 latent). It generates 80 novel views for single-image-to-3D, and 480-960 views for few-view reconstruction (depending on dataset/trajectory). (2) The reconstruction pipeline distills these into a persistent 3D representation — a Zip-NeRF (NeRF), which renders in real time; 3D Gaussian Splatting (3DGS) is also mentioned as an alternative reconstruction target. The final 3D model is persistent and explorable / real-time renderable. No explicit FPS number is given for the final render beyond "real-time." Source: https://arxiv.org/html/2405.10314v1
- **Structure:** Multi-view latent diffusion model (LDM) built on a 2D diffusion U-Net backbone, initialized from a pretrained text-to-image latent diffusion model (similar to the Imagen/latent-diffusion line). Key architectural modification: 3D self-attention is added after the existing 2D residual blocks — "2D in space and 1D across images" (i.e., spatiotemporal attention connecting all views in the batch), applied only at feature-map resolutions <=32x32 to balance quality and compute. This is essentially a video-diffusion-style architecture, but camera-pose raymap embeddings replace time embeddings (concatenated channel-wise to the latents), adding only a few extra convolution input channels. It conditions on a variable number of clean (noise-free) input-view latents and denoises the target-view latents jointly. A frozen VAE encoder/decoder maps between 512x512 images and 64x64x8 latents. The downstream reconstruction is a separate robust Zip-NeRF (or 3DGS) optimizer, not a neural network trained jointly. Source: https://arxiv.org/html/2405.10314v1
- **Training objectives:** Diffusion model: standard denoising diffusion objective (epsilon/v-prediction style latent diffusion loss) computed on the target-view latents only (input/conditional view latents are kept clean and excluded from the loss). Classifier-free guidance is enabled by dropping the conditional images and camera poses with probability 0.1 during training (CFG weight 3 at sampling). 3D reconstruction stage (Zip-NeRF, optimized per-scene, not learned weights): photometric reconstruction loss plus an LPIPS perceptual loss (weight ~0.25-1.0, found critical for texture/geometry under imperfect generated views), a distortion loss, an interlevel loss, and L2 weight regularization (inherited from Zip-NeRF). It also uses a distance-based per-view weighting that down-weights generated views far from observed views, using a Gaussian kernel w proportional to exp(-b*s^2) with b annealed from 0 up to 15. Source: https://arxiv.org/html/2405.10314v1
- **Data:** Multi-view diffusion model trained on four datasets sampled with equal probability: Objaverse (synthetic 3D objects, rendered), CO3D (real object-centric captures), RealEstate10K (real indoor/scene video with camera poses), and MVImgNet (real multi-view object/scene images). This mixes synthetic (Objaverse) and real (CO3D, RealEstate10K, MVImgNet) data. The base text-to-image model was pretrained on web-scale image datasets. Exact counts of clips/scenes/hours used for CAT3D fine-tuning are not reported beyond naming the datasets. Source: https://arxiv.org/html/2405.10314v1
- **Training stages:** Three conceptual stages. (1) Pretraining: the backbone is a text-to-image latent diffusion model pretrained on web-scale images (inherited, not trained here). (2) Multi-view fine-tuning of the diffusion model in two sub-phases on 8 total views: ~1M iterations with 1 conditional + 7 target views, then ~0.4M additional iterations mixing the 1+7 and 3+5 (conditional+target) configurations — total 1.4M iterations, batch size 128, learning rate 5e-5. (3) Per-scene 3D reconstruction (Zip-NeRF/3DGS) at inference time, optimized on the generated views (not a learned-weights training stage). No RLHF or distillation stage. Source: https://arxiv.org/html/2405.10314v1
- **Model size:** Multi-view diffusion model: ~850M parameters total (the paper notes this is smaller than IM-3D's 4.3B and SV3D's 1.5B, and that the multi-view raymap conditioning adds only a few additional convolution channels to the backbone). Source: https://arxiv.org/html/2405.10314v1
- **Training compute:** Diffusion-model training hardware/wall-clock time: not reported (the paper gives iteration count, batch size, and learning rate, but not GPU/TPU type, count, or training days). Inference/reconstruction compute IS reported: novel-view generation takes ~5 seconds to produce 80 views on 16 A100 GPUs; the synthetic-view sampling and 3D reconstruction also run on 16 A100 GPUs, taking ~4 minutes for few-view reconstruction and ~55 seconds for single-image-to-3D. So 16x A100 is for inference, not training. Source: https://arxiv.org/html/2405.10314v1
- **Key results:** Creates a full 3D scene in as little as ~1 minute, an order of magnitude faster than prior work (e.g., ReconFusion). Single-image-to-3D: CLIP score ~88.54 +/- 8.6 in ~1 minute, vs ImageDream 83.77 +/- 5.2 in ~120 minutes. Few-view (3-view) on mip-NeRF 360: PSNR 16.62 / SSIM 0.377 / LPIPS 0.515, outperforming ReconFusion (15.50 / 0.358 / 0.585). Outperforms existing methods on single-image and few-view 3D scene creation across benchmarks (mip-NeRF 360, CO3D, RealEstate10K, etc.). Generation: 5s for 80 views on 16 A100s. Decoupling generation from reconstruction is the key speed/quality lever. Source: https://arxiv.org/html/2405.10314v1 , https://cat3d.github.io/
- **Verification (size/compute/data), confidence high:** Verified all three claims against the primary source (arXiv HTML v1, https://arxiv.org/html/2405.10314v1).

SIZE: CONFIRMED. Paper states verbatim: "Our latent diffusion model has 850M parameters, smaller than existing approaches built on video diffusion models such as IM-3D (4.3B) and SV3D (1.5B)." Also: "Our model adds minimal additional parameters to the backbone model: just a few additional convolution channels at the input layer to handle conditioning information." Claim is exact.

## 8. Recent benchmarks for evaluating world models

How current world models are being measured (2024–2026), including the newest releases.


### 8.1 WorldScore
*Stanford University (Stanford Vision and Learning Lab / SVL) — Haoyi Duan, Hong-Xing (Koven) Yu, Sirui Chen, Li Fei-Fei, Jiajun Wu · First arXiv submission 2025-04-01 (v1); v2 revised 2025-11-29. Presented at ICCV 2025. Live leaderboard updated through 2025-2026.*  
[haoyi-duan.github.io/WorldScore](https://haoyi-duan.github.io/WorldScore/)

- **Measures:** WorldScore is the first unified benchmark for "world generation" (world models). It reframes world generation as a sequence of next-scene generation tasks driven by explicit camera-trajectory and layout instructions, which lets it evaluate 3D scene-generation, 4D scene-generation, and video-generation models on a single common axis. It jointly measures three families of abilities of world models: (1) Controllability — whether the model follows the instructed camera motion, requested object layout, and scene content; (2) Quality — geometric/visual fidelity and consistency of the generated world across frames; and (3) Dynamics — how well dynamic (moving) content is generated. The benchmark's motivation is that prior single-scene video-quality benchmarks rate very different world models as similar, missing controllability and cross-scene consistency.
- **Tasks:** Evaluation is over a curated dataset of 3,000 test examples spanning diverse worlds: static and dynamic scenes, indoor and outdoor, photorealistic and stylized. The core task is decomposed into a chain of next-scene generation steps: given an initial scene plus explicit camera-trajectory specifications (and object/content layout instructions), the model must extend the world to the next scene. Tasks vary by scene length (including stress-testing long-sequence generation), domain (indoor vs outdoor), motion (static vs dynamic), and visual style (photorealistic vs stylized).
- **Metrics:** Ten metrics across three dimensions. Controllability: (1) Camera Controllability — adherence to instructed camera trajectory via rotation/translation error (estimated with a SLAM/pose pipeline, e.g. DROID-SLAM); (2) Object Controllability — success rate of requested objects appearing in the generated scene; (3) Content Alignment — match between generated scene and text prompt (CLIPScore). Quality: (4) 3D Consistency — geometric stability across frames via reprojection error; (5) Photometric Consistency — texture/appearance stability via optical-flow analysis; (6) Style Consistency — artistic-style preservation via Gram-matrix differences between frames; (7) Subjective Quality — perceptual quality combining CLIP-IQA+ and CLIP-Aesthetic, aligned to human judgment. Dynamics: (8) Motion Accuracy — whether motion occurs in the correct/specified regions; (9) Motion Magnitude — amount of motion via optical flow between consecutive frames; (10) Motion Smoothness — temporal coherence/fluidity using frame-interpolation as reference. Aggregates: WorldScore-Static = arithmetic mean of the controllability + quality metrics (for static models); WorldScore-Dynamic = adds the dynamics metrics on top of controllability + quality (for dynamic/video models).
- **Models evaluated:** The paper evaluated ~20 representative open- and closed-source models across three categories. 3D scene-generation: WonderWorld (72.69 static), LucidDreamer (70.40), WonderJourney (63.75), Text2Room (62.10), InvisibleStitch (61.12), SceneScape (50.73). Closed-source video: Gen-3 (60.71 static / 57.58 dynamic), Hailuo/MiniMax (57.55). Open-source video: CogVideoX-I2V (62.15), LTX-Video (55.44), Allegro (55.31), EasyAnimate (52.85), VideoCrafter2 (52.57), DynamiCrafter (52.09), CogVideoX-T2V (54.18), VideoCrafter1-I2V (50.47), VideoCrafter1-T2V (47.10), T2V-Turbo (45.65), Vchitect-2.0 (42.28). 4D generation: 4D-fy (27.98). Headline ranking: 3D scene-generation models top WorldScore-Static, led by WonderWorld (72.69); among video models, open-source CogVideoX-I2V (62.15) edged out closed-source Gen-3. The live leaderboard has since added newer entries, with Voyager (HunyuanWorld/Tencent) topping WorldScore-Static at ~77.62 (and ~54.53 dynamic), ahead of WonderWorld. Note: exact per-model numbers track WorldScore-Static unless a dynamic score is given.
- **Key findings:** 1) No single model class wins on everything: 3D scene-generation models dominate WorldScore-Static (better camera controllability and 3D/visual consistency than the best video models), while video models are needed for dynamics. 2) Camera controllability is the central weakness of video-generation models — even top video models score far below 3D methods on following instructed camera trajectories. 3) Open-source can beat closed-source: CogVideoX-I2V scored higher on WorldScore-Static than both evaluated closed-source video models. 4) Motion trade-offs: larger motion magnitude tends to reduce motion smoothness, and motion magnitude correlates only weakly with motion accuracy. 5) Video models degrade sharply on long-sequence tasks and on outdoor scenes (vs indoor). 6) T2V vs I2V split: text-to-video models tend to score higher on controllability while image-to-video models score higher on quality. 7) Extending 3D models into the 4D/dynamic regime remains hard (4D-fy scored lowest at 27.98). Overall the benchmark concludes current world models each have category-specific gaps — pointing to controllability, long-horizon consistency, outdoor generation, and unified 3D/4D dynamics as open challenges.
- **Sources:** [haoyi-duan.github.io/WorldScore](https://haoyi-duan.github.io/WorldScore/) · [arxiv.org/abs/2504.00983](https://arxiv.org/abs/2504.00983) · [arxiv.org/html/2504.00983v2](https://arxiv.org/html/2504.00983v2) · [arxiv.org/pdf/2504.00983](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2504.00983) · [github.com/haoyi-duan/WorldScore](https://github.com/haoyi-duan/WorldScore) · [huggingface.co/spaces/Howieeeee/WorldScore_Leaderboard](https://huggingface.co/spaces/Howieeeee/WorldScore_Leaderboard) · [openaccess.thecvf.com/content/ICCV2025/html/Duan_WorldScore_A_Unified_Evaluation_Benchmark_for_World_Generation_ICCV_2025_paper.html](https://openaccess.thecvf.com/content/ICCV2025/html/Duan_WorldScore_A_Unified_Evaluation_Benchmark_for_World_Generation_ICCV_2025_paper.html) · [x.com/drfeifei/status/1909407386902970378](https://x.com/drfeifei/status/1909407386902970378)

### 8.2 WorldModelBench: Judging Video Generation Models As World Models
*WorldModelBench Team — authors affiliated with UC Berkeley, NVIDIA, MIT, and UC San Diego (Dacheng Li, Yunhao Fang, Yukang Chen, Shuo Yang, Shiyi Cao, Justin Wong, Michael Luo, Xiaolong Wang, Hongxu Yin, Joseph E. Gonzalez, Ion Stoica, Song Han, Yao Lu). Presented as an oral/poster paper at the WorldModelBench Workshop @ CVPR 2025. Note: the report's "Stanford" attribution is imprecise — the leading authors are primarily UC Berkeley (Gonzalez, Stoica) and NVIDIA, with MIT (Song Han) and UC San Diego (Xiaolong Wang); NVIDIA affiliation is accurate. · arXiv v1 submitted 2025-02-28 (paper dated Feb 27, 2025); presented at WorldModelBench Workshop, CVPR 2025 (June 2025). Brand-new 2025 benchmark.*  
[arxiv.org/abs/2502.20694](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.20694)

- **Measures:** WorldModelBench evaluates VIDEO GENERATION models specifically in their role as "world models" for application-driven, decision-making domains (robotics, autonomous driving, etc.), rather than as generic video-quality generators. It targets three world-modeling abilities that prior video benchmarks (e.g., VBench) miss: (1) Instruction Following — whether the generated video actually performs the action/task specified by the text or image-plus-text condition; (2) Common Sense — whether the video is visually and temporally coherent/plausible (frame quality and motion coherence over time); and (3) Physical Adherence — whether the generated dynamics obey real-world physical laws (no spontaneous motion, no irregular deformation/mass change, realistic fluids, no object inter-penetration, no gravity violations like floating). It supports both text-to-video (T2V) and image-to-video (I2V) models. A core motivation is that conventional metrics correlate poorly with physical correctness (the paper reports VBench's correlation with physics adherence drops to ~0.28).
- **Tasks:** 350 condition prompts (image+text and text-only pairs) spanning 7 application-driven domains and 56 subdomains. The 7 domains are: Autonomous Driving, Robotics, Industry, Human Activities, Gaming (simulation/gaming), Animation, and Natural (natural scenes). Each prompt asks a model to generate a video that completes a specified action/scenario in that domain; generated videos are then scored on the three criteria. The benchmark provides a held-out human-labeled set used to train/validate an automated judge: ~4,421 training videos and 713 test videos with annotations.
- **Metrics:** Three sub-scores combine into a single composite score on a 0-10 scale (3 + 2 + 5):
(1) Instruction Following — 0-3 ordinal scale (0 = subject absent/stationary; 1 = subject moves but fails the intended action; 2 = partial task completion; 3 = full, accurate task completion).
(2) Common Sense — 0-2 scale, split into Frame-wise/aesthetic quality (0-1) + Temporal coherence quality (0-1).
(3) Physical Adherence — 0-5 scale, the sum of 5 binary (0/1) physical-law checks: Newton's First Law (no motion without external force), Mass Conservation / Solid Mechanics (no irregular deformation), Fluid Mechanics (no unnatural liquid flow), Impenetrability/Non-penetration (objects don't pass through each other), and Gravity (objects don't float/violate gravity).
Evaluation is automated by a fine-tuned 2B-parameter multimodal judge (built on VILA-2B), trained on the human labels; it predicts world-modeling violations with ~8.6% higher average accuracy (equivalently a lower error rate, ~4.1% average prediction error) than GPT-4o. The benchmark is grounded in ~67K crowdsourced human labels (8,336 complete votes from 65 annotators; 87.1% of votes within an absolute score difference of 2).
- **Models evaluated:** 14 frontier video generation models, both closed- and open-source. Headline composite-score ranking (0-10): KLING 8.82 (best overall), Minimax 8.59, Mochi-official 8.37, Runway 8.08, Luma 7.72; best open-source: Mochi 7.62 and OpenSoraPlan-T2V 7.61; lowest performers: OpenSora-T2V 6.11 and OpenSora-I2V 5.83 (worst). General pattern: closed-source models outperform open-source, and image-to-video (I2V) variants substantially underperform their text-to-video (T2V) counterparts.
- **Key findings:** Current video generation models are still weak as world models. Even the top model (KLING) only correctly completes the specified task in ~61% of videos, showing instruction following is far from solved. ~12% of generated videos violate the mass conservation (solid mechanics) law, indicating frequent physics failures. Image-to-video models lag well behind text-to-video models. Conventional video-quality metrics (e.g., VBench) correlate poorly with physical correctness (correlation drops to ~0.28), motivating a dedicated world-model benchmark. The fine-tuned 2B judge beats GPT-4o at detecting violations (~8.6% higher accuracy) while being far smaller/cheaper. Finally, the authors show that using the benchmark's human-aligned signal as a reward to fine-tune a generator (reward maximization / aligning with human annotations) noticeably improves measured world-modeling capability — demonstrating the benchmark is useful not just for ranking but for improving models.
- **Sources:** [arxiv.org/abs/2502.20694](https://arxiv.org/abs/2502.20694) · [arxiv.org/html/2502.20694v1](https://arxiv.org/html/2502.20694v1) · [arxiv.org/pdf/2502.20694](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2502.20694) · [worldmodelbench-team.github.io](https://worldmodelbench-team.github.io/) · [github.com/WorldModelBench-Team/WorldModelBench](https://github.com/WorldModelBench-Team/WorldModelBench) · [openreview.net/forum?id=a3hafrDzuA](https://openreview.net/forum?id=a3hafrDzuA) · [worldmodelbench.github.io](https://worldmodelbench.github.io/)

### 8.3 WM-ABench (World Model Atomic Benchmark)
*Maitrix.org — UC San Diego, Johns Hopkins University, University of Michigan, EPFL (lead authors Qiyue Gao, Xinyu Pi et al.; senior authors Zhiting Hu, Lianhui Qin, Ziqiao Ma, Tianmin Shu) · arXiv v1 released 2025-06-27 (paper: "Do Vision-Language Models Have Internal World Models? Towards an Atomic Evaluation"; accepted to ACL 2025 Findings)*  
[arxiv.org/abs/2506.21876](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.21876)

- **Measures:** WM-ABench performs an "atomic" evaluation of whether Vision-Language Models (VLMs) function as internal world models, decomposing world modeling into two stages and 23 fine-grained dimensions. PERCEPTION (decoding the current world state, 5 categories): Visual (color, shape, material), Spatial (spatial relation, spatial vacancy, spatial occupancy, spatial positioning), Temporal (temporal positioning, temporal extension), Quantitative (discrete quantity, continuous quantity, relative quantity), and Motion (motion speed, motion direction, motion identification, motion trajectory). PREDICTION (simulating future world states, 3 categories): Mechanistic Simulation (single forward step), Transitive Inference (chaining/multi-step), and Compositional Inference (combining independent factors). The design uses controlled counterfactuals where only one dimension varies per data point, so it can also detect whether VLMs form disentangled vs. entangled internal representations.
- **Tasks:** Built on 6 simulated/physics environments (THREEDWorld, Carla, ManiSkill, Physion, etc.) spanning outdoor navigation, indoor navigation, collision (collide), sliding (slide), dropping (drop), and robot-arm manipulation (push, lift, drop). Concrete task families: Perception tasks query the 15 perception sub-dimensions above from rendered scenes/videos. Prediction tasks: Mechanistic Simulation = Outdoor Nav, Indoor Nav, Collide, Slide, Drop, Robot Arm Drop, Robot Arm Lift, Robot Arm Push; Transitive Inference = Outdoor Nav, Indoor Nav, Robot Arm Push-Pick, Robot Arm Pick-Rotate; Compositional Inference = Collide, Robot Arm Push, Robot Arm Lift. Reported as ~100,000+ evaluation instances across 660 experiments; controlled counterfactual variants isolate single dimensions.
- **Metrics:** Primary metric: Accuracy (%) per dimension and aggregated per category (multiple-choice / state-discrimination questions; many motion tasks have a 25% / 4-way random baseline). Disentanglement metric: "Standardized Relative Entanglement (s-RE)" — the normalized/average relative performance deviation of a target dimension when an unrelated attribute is perturbed (higher s-RE = more entangled, non-disentangled representations; e.g., color-quantity s-RE ranges from ~5% for Gemini-1.5-Pro to ~17% for Qwen2.5-VL). Human baselines are collected per category for reference (e.g., spatial 94.75%, temporal 84.5%, vision 92.67%, motion 92.75%, quantity 98.67%).
- **Models evaluated:** 15 VLMs (commercial + open-source). Open-source: Qwen2-VL-72B, Qwen2.5-VL-72B, InternVL2.5-78B, LLaVA-OneVision (72B), NVILA, Llama-3.2-Vision-90B. Commercial: GPT-4o, GPT-4o-mini, GPT-4.5-preview, OpenAI o3, Gemini-1.5-pro, Gemini-1.5-flash, Gemini-2.5-pro, Claude 3.5 Sonnet, Claude 3.7 Sonnet. Headline rankings: among the broadly-evaluated set, Qwen2-VL reported the highest overall perception accuracy (~67.7%) and highest prediction accuracy (~47.5%); among frontier reasoning models, Gemini-2.5-pro led spatial perception (~84.75% avg) and o3 led motion perception (~70.63%) and mechanistic simulation (~75.3%). All models remain well below human performance.
- **Key findings:** Current VLMs are NOT yet robust internal world models. (1) All 15 VLMs fall far behind humans across every category. (2) Internal representations are entangled/non-disentangled rather than physically grounded — color and shape are the dominant confounds, leaking into unrelated judgments (e.g., models infer "blue objects move faster than green ones," or color/shape bias size and speed estimates), quantified by s-RE. (3) Motion is a severe weakness: almost all models perform at near-random accuracy when distinguishing motion trajectories; e.g., GPT-4o ~38.86% and Gemini-1.5-pro ~42.53% motion-perception average vs. ~92.75% human. (4) A clear perception-prediction gap: even when a model perfectly perceives the current state, prediction of the next physical state improves only marginally, indicating VLMs rely on shortcuts/pattern-matching rather than simulating physical dynamics. Frontier models (o3, Gemini-2.5-pro) improve but still trail humans, implying world modeling is a distinct, unsolved capability.
- **Sources:** [arxiv.org/abs/2506.21876](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.21876) · [arxiv.org/html/2506.21876v1](https://arxiv.org/html/2506.21876v1) · [wm-abench.maitrix.org](https://wm-abench.maitrix.org/) · [huggingface.co/datasets/maitrix-org/WM-ABench](https://huggingface.co/datasets/maitrix-org/WM-ABench) · [huggingface.co/papers/2506.21876](https://huggingface.co/papers/2506.21876) · [openreview.net/forum?id=tpPv3ayoqo](https://openreview.net/forum?id=tpPv3ayoqo)

### 8.4 WorldSimBench: Towards Video Generation Models as World Simulators
*CUHK-Shenzhen, Shanghai AI Laboratory, Beihang University, University of Hong Kong (authors include Yiran Qin, Zhelun Shi, Jing Shao, Lei Bai, Ruimao Zhang, Wanli Ouyang). GigaAI is commonly associated via the World Simulator line; the four listed affiliations are the formal ones on the paper. · arXiv v1 released 23 October 2024 (arXiv:2410.18072). Accepted to ICML 2025 (poster).*  
[arxiv.org/abs/2410.18072](https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.18072)

- **Measures:** WorldSimBench evaluates "World Simulators" — the top tier (Stage S3) of a proposed hierarchy of Predictive Models classified by output modality (S0 text -> S1 image -> S2 video -> S3 actionable video). It measures two complementary ability classes of video-generation world models from an embodied perspective: (1) Explicit Perceptual ability — visual fidelity and instruction/physics adherence of generated videos as judged by human-aligned preference; and (2) Implicit Manipulative ability — "video-action consistency," i.e. whether a generated situation-aware video can be translated into correct control signals that actually complete embodied tasks in closed-loop simulation. Three capability axes are scored: Visual Quality, Condition Consistency, and Embodiment (physical-rule / embodied-factor adherence). It targets what prior benchmarks (VBench, EvalCrafter, EgoPlan-Bench, MMWorld, VAB, LEGO, AgentBench) miss: action-level, embodied, closed-loop evaluation rather than aesthetic feature-similarity or text-only planning.
- **Tasks:** Two evaluation tracks across three embodied scenarios: Open-Ended Embodied Environment (OE, testbed = Minecraft), Autonomous Driving (AD, outdoor driving / CARLA-style sim), and Robot Manipulation (RM, CALVIN-style tabletop arm). EXPLICIT PERCEPTUAL EVALUATION: 8 video models generate clips from per-scenario instruction-prompt lists (e.g. "Move the robot arm on top of pink cloth", "Execute a right turn at the upcoming intersection", "Collect sand on the plain"); a trained Human Preference Evaluator scores them on ~20 fine-grained dimensions. IMPLICIT MANIPULATIVE EVALUATION (closed-loop): a high-level planner decomposes an embodied task into sub-tasks; the world simulator generates a predicted video from the current observation + instruction; a pre-trained inverse-dynamics model (IDM) or goal-based video-to-action policy converts the video into actions; the agent executes, the video is re-sampled after a fixed timestep, and this loop repeats. Concrete OE tasks include collect wood / collect dirt / collect seeds / explore / vertical digging (Minecraft); AD measures route/driving/infraction behavior; RM uses sequential arm sub-tasks (e.g. lift red block from cabinet, store in drawer).
- **Metrics:** EXPLICIT (Human Preference Evaluator, 1-5 fine-grained scores over 20 dimensions across the 3 scenarios, grouped as): Visual Quality — Aesthetics, Foreground Consistency, Background Consistency; Condition Consistency — Instruction Alignment, Scenario Alignment; Embodiment — Velocity, Trajectory, Embodied Interaction, Perspectivity (perspective consistency), Key Element, Safety. (Per-scenario subset, e.g. OE: Background/Foreground Consistency, Instruction/Scenario Alignment, Velocity, Trajectory, Embodied Interaction; AD: Aesthetics, Instruction Alignment, Perspectivity, Trajectory, Key Element, Safety; RM: Aesthetics, Background/Foreground Consistency, Instruction Alignment, Perspectivity, Trajectory, Embodied Interaction.) IMPLICIT: task success rate from simulation-environment monitors (closed-loop), plus scenario-specific embodied metrics — OE/Minecraft: travel distance, dig depth, item/inventory counts (e.g. "collect dirt" score, "travel distance"); AD: route completion / driving score / infraction score (collisions, red-light and offroad violations); RM: success rate over trials. Headline reporting uses an overall average score combining these.
- **Models evaluated:** 8 popular open video-generation models: Open-Sora (OpenSora), Open-Sora-Plan (T2V variant), Open-Sora-Plan (TI2V variant), Lavie, ModelScope, AnimateDiff, DynamiCrafter, and EasyAnimate. Headline: OpenSora ranked best overall (highest overall average ~27.80), excelling on Minecraft tasks (collect dirt 70.20, travel distance 339.87); Open-Sora-Plan and ModelScope were competitive; AnimateDiff and EasyAnimate were the weakest.
- **Key findings:** (1) Existing benchmarks cannot evaluate highly-embodied, action-capable predictive models; WorldSimBench is the first to evaluate S3 "World Simulators" at the action level in closed-loop sim. (2) Even the best current video generators are weak World Simulators: they fail to reliably capture physical rules, plausible object interactions, 3D/perspective consistency, and instruction-aligned control, so generated videos translate poorly into correct actions. (3) Perceptual visual quality does not guarantee manipulative usefulness — models can look good yet drive tasks poorly, motivating the dual (explicit + implicit) framework. (4) OpenSora leads but absolute embodied success remains low, leaving large headroom. (5) Contributions enabling the field: the HF-Embodied Dataset (35,701 tuples / entries of fine-grained human feedback with multi-dimensional scores and written rationales across 3 scenarios and 20 dimensions) and the trained Human Preference Evaluator, which also support future alignment/RLHF of video models.

### 8.5 PhyWorldBench
*NVIDIA (Cosmos Lab) and UC Santa Cruz et al. (Jing Gu, Xian Liu, Yu Zeng, Ashwin Nagarajan, Fangrui Zhu, Daniel Hong, Yue Fan, Qianqi Yan, Kaiwen Zhou, Ming-Yu Liu, Xin Eric Wang). NOTE: The task brief attributed this to "University of Maryland," but the primary sources do not list UMD; affiliations center on NVIDIA Cosmos Lab and UC Santa Cruz. The UMD attribution appears to be a mismatch and should be corrected in the study report. · First arXiv submission 2025-07-17 (v1); revised through 2026-05-26 (v3). Accepted to ICLR 2026 (oral).*  
[arxiv.org/abs/2507.13428](https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.13428)

- **Measures:** Physical realism of text-to-video (T2V) "world model" generators: whether generated videos obey real-world physics. It probes two distinct abilities per video: (1) Semantic Adherence (SA) — does the video render what the prompt asked for (correct object count, correct action depicted); and (2) Physical Commonsense (PC) — does the depicted motion/interaction obey physical laws. It spans difficulty levels from fundamental single-phenomenon physics (kinematics, energy conservation, fluids, light/shadow, deformation) through composite multi-body/agent scenarios, up to deliberately physics-violating "anti-physics" prompts that test whether a model truly understands physics versus pattern-matching its training distribution.
- **Tasks:** 1,050 curated prompts organized as 10 physics categories x 5 subcategories x 7 scenarios x 3 prompt variations. The 10 categories sit in three tiers: FUNDAMENTAL PHYSICS (6 categories): Object Motion/Kinematics, Interaction Dynamics, Energy Conservation, Fluid/Particle Dynamics, Lighting/Shadows, Deformations/Elasticity. COMPOSITE PHYSICS (3 categories): Scale/Proportions, Rigid Body Dynamics, Human/Animal Motion. ANTI-PHYSICS (1 category): intentionally impossible scenarios such as defying gravity, perpetual motion, objects phasing through each other, time reversal, and duplication. Prompts come in three phrasing variations (e.g., event-only vs. physics-augmented vs. detailed) to test whether explicitly stating the physics helps the model follow it.
- **Metrics:** Two evaluation tracks. (1) HUMAN EVALUATION: each video scored on two binary (0/1) axes — Semantic Adherence (SA: correct object count and correct action) and Physical Commonsense (PC: adherence to real-world physics). Run on Amazon Mechanical Turk with 3 independent annotators per video, majority vote. The headline "success rate" = fraction of videos satisfying BOTH SA and PC. (2) MLLM ZERO-SHOT AUTO-EVALUATION via the proposed Context-Aware Prompt (CAP) method: a GPT-o1-based two-step chain-of-thought judge — step 1 describes the video (objects, events, observations); step 2 does structured category-specific reasoning about physics violations. Explicitly telling the judge the video is AI-generated improves accuracy. CAP correlation with human labels: ROC-AUC ~80.3 for SA and ~75.1 for PC. They also benchmark other MLLM judges (GPT-4o, GPT-o1, Gemini-2.0-flash, Qwen-VL-2.0).
- **Models evaluated:** 12 state-of-the-art T2V models. PROPRIETARY (5): Sora-Turbo, Gen-3 (Runway), Kling 1.6, Pika 2.0, Luma. OPEN-SOURCE (7): Hunyuan 720p, Open-Sora 2.0, Open-Sora-Plan 1.3, CogVideoX-14B, Step-Video-T2V, Wanx-2.1, LTX-Video. HEADLINE RANKING (success rate = both SA and PC satisfied): Pika 2.0 best overall at 0.262; best open-source model Wanx-2.1 at 0.189; worst LTX-Video at 0.062. Even the best model satisfies both axes on only ~26% of videos.
- **Key findings:** Current T2V/world models struggle badly with physics despite high visual quality: the best model (Pika 2.0) jointly satisfies semantics and physics on only ~26% of prompts. Models prioritize cinematic aesthetics and photorealism over physical plausibility. Performance degrades sharply as scenes add multiple objects and complex interactions (composite physics), and collapses on anti-physics prompts — strong evidence that models replicate training-data patterns rather than understanding physical laws, since training distributions favor everyday scenes. Prompt engineering helps only partially: explicitly adding physics statements usually increases physics-following ability, but more detailed/elaborate prompt refinement does not reliably improve and can hurt results. Methodologically, a standard MLLM cannot reliably judge physics zero-shot, but the proposed CAP (context-aware, two-step chain-of-thought, told the video is AI-generated) substantially improves agreement with human judgments (ROC-AUC ~80 SA / ~75 PC), offering a scalable automatic evaluator.
- **Sources:** [arxiv.org/abs/2507.13428](https://arxiv.org/abs/2507.13428) · [arxiv.org/html/2507.13428v1](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.13428v1) · [arxiv.org/pdf/2507.13428](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2507.13428) · [research.nvidia.com/labs/cosmos-lab/phyworldbench](https://research.nvidia.com/labs/cosmos-lab/phyworldbench/) · [github.com/g-jing/phy-world-bench](https://github.com/g-jing/phy-world-bench) · [github.com/ashwin-333/phy-world-bench](https://github.com/ashwin-333/phy-world-bench)

### 8.6 Physics-IQ
*Google DeepMind, INSAIT (Sofia University), University of Toronto / Vector Institute · Released 2025-01-14 on arXiv (v1 titled "Do generative video models learn physical principles from watching videos?"; revised v3 2025-02-27 retitled "Do generative video models understand physical principles?"). Published at WACV 2026. Leaderboard remains live and updated through 2026 (latest entries May 2026).*  
[physics-iq.github.io](https://physics-iq.github.io)

- **Measures:** Whether generative (text/image/video-conditioned) video models possess genuine physical understanding rather than merely producing visually realistic frames — i.e., whether they have learned a predictive "world model" of physics from video. It probes understanding of five physics domains: solid mechanics, fluid dynamics, optics, thermodynamics, and magnetism. The central question is whether a model, given the start of a real scene, can predict how it will physically evolve. A separate axis measures visual realism (whether outputs can fool a multimodal LLM) so the two can be compared.
- **Tasks:** A real-world dataset of 396 videos covering 66 distinct physical scenarios. Each scenario is filmed twice (two takes) from three camera perspectives (left/center/right) at 3840x2160, 30fps, 8 seconds. The two real takes of a scenario differ only by physical randomness and define the upper-bound score. Scenarios are concrete physical interactions (e.g., fluids pouring/splashing, objects colliding/falling, balls rolling, paint mixing, light/optics, heat, magnets attracting). Models are conditioned on the real beginning of each scene at a designated "switch frame" and must continue/predict the physical future. Two conditioning protocols: (1) image-to-video (i2v) — condition on a single frame; (2) multiframe (v2v) — condition on up to ~3 seconds of real frames. The generated continuation is compared against the real continuation.
- **Metrics:** Four per-scenario metrics are combined into a single normalized "Physics-IQ score" (0-100%): (1) Spatial IoU — "where does action happen?": IoU of binary motion maps (location of movement only). (2) Spatiotemporal IoU — "where and when does action happen?": extends Spatial IoU to per-frame motion masks so motion must occur at the right place AND right time. (3) Weighted spatial IoU — "where and how much action happens?": averages motion magnitude over time (rather than max), distinguishing transient vs. sustained/repeated motion. (4) MSE (Mean Squared Error) — "how does action happen?": pixel-level error between generated and real frames (visual/appearance fidelity). The Physics-IQ score is normalized so that two real takes of the same scenario (differing only by physical randomness) score ~100%; a perfect physics-aware model approaches 100%. Separately, an MLLM realism score uses Gemini 1.5 Pro in a forced-choice "which video is generated?" test; reported as the model's ability to fool the MLLM (higher = more realistic/harder to detect).
- **Models evaluated:** Original paper evaluated 6 models across i2v and/or multiframe: VideoPoet (multiframe & i2v), Lumiere (multiframe & i2v), Runway Gen-3 (i2v), Stable Video Diffusion (i2v), Pika 1.0 (i2v), and Sora (i2v). Headline Physics-IQ ranking (best to worst): VideoPoet (multiframe) 29.5% — best; Lumiere (multiframe) 23.0%; Runway Gen-3 (i2v) 22.8%; VideoPoet (i2v) 20.3%; Lumiere (i2v) 19.0%; Stable Video Diffusion, Pika 1.0, and Sora lowest (Sora i2v ~8.7-10%). All scores far below the ~100% real-video upper bound. The live leaderboard has since added newer world models; as of May 2026 the top entries are Cosmos3-Super + WMReward (BoN) (v2v) 63.4%, Magi-1 + WMReward (BoN) (v2v) 62.6%, and Cosmos3-Super (v2v) 59.7%, with the best i2v being Cosmos3-Super + WMReward at 48.9%.
- **Key findings:** Current generative video models have severely limited physical understanding: even the best original model (VideoPoet multiframe) scored only 29.5% vs. the ~100% real-video upper bound. Crucially, visual realism does NOT imply (and does not correlate with) physical understanding — the model that best fooled the MLLM on realism (Sora, ~55.6% MLLM realism) was among the worst on actual physics (Physics-IQ ~8.7-10%). Multiframe conditioning outperforms single-frame i2v. Some physics is partially learnable from observation (fluid dynamics tends to work better than solid mechanics), suggesting learning physics purely from video pixels is possible but far from solved. Conclusion: today's video models largely replicate visual patterns rather than acquiring genuine world-model physics. Newer 2025-2026 models on the live leaderboard (e.g., Cosmos, Magi-1) have roughly doubled scores (~60%) but still fall well short of real-video physical fidelity.
- **Sources:** [physics-iq.github.io](https://physics-iq.github.io) · [arxiv.org/abs/2501.09038](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.09038) · [arxiv.org/html/2501.09038v1](https://arxiv.org/html/2501.09038v1) · [github.com/google-deepmind/physics-IQ-benchmark](https://github.com/google-deepmind/physics-IQ-benchmark) · [openaccess.thecvf.com/content/WACV2026/papers/Motamed_Do_Generative_Video_Models_Understand_Physical_Principles_WACV_2026_paper.pdf](https://openaccess.thecvf.com/content/WACV2026/papers/Motamed_Do_Generative_Video_Models_Understand_Physical_Principles_WACV_2026_paper.pdf) · [www.themoonlight.io/en/review/do-generative-video-models-understand-physical-principles](https://www.themoonlight.io/en/review/do-generative-video-models-understand-physical-principles)

### 8.7 PhyGenBench (with PhyGenEval evaluation framework)
*OpenGVLab (Shanghai AI Laboratory), with collaborators; paper "Towards World Simulator: Crafting Physical Commonsense-Based Benchmark for Video Generation" · arXiv v1 posted 2024-10-07 (arXiv:2410.05363); accepted to ICML 2025 (per the official GitHub repo title "[ICML2025]")*  
[github.com/OpenGVLab/PhyGenBench](https://github.com/OpenGVLab/PhyGenBench)

- **Measures:** Whether text-to-video (T2V) generation models — treated as candidate "world simulators" — understand and correctly implement physical commonsense / physical laws when generating video from text. It probes the world-model-relevant ability to produce physically plausible dynamics rather than just visually appealing or semantically-on-topic clips. Physical commonsense is assessed across four fundamental domains spanning 27 distinct physical laws: (1) Mechanics (gravity, buoyancy, solid pressure, atmospheric pressure, elasticity, friction, surface tension), (2) Optics (reflection, refraction, scattering, dispersion, interference/diffraction, rectilinear/straight-line propagation), (3) Thermal (solidification, melting, liquefaction, boiling, deposition, sublimation), and (4) Material Properties (physical properties such as color, hardness, solubility, combustibility, flame reaction; plus chemical properties such as acidity, redox/oxidizability, dehydrating property).
- **Tasks:** 160 carefully crafted (validated) text prompts covering the 27 physical laws across the 4 domains. Each prompt describes a scenario whose correct rendering requires a specific physical law (e.g., an object's expected motion under gravity/buoyancy, light reflection/refraction, phase transitions like melting/boiling, or material reactions like combustion/color change). A T2V model generates a video for each prompt, and the generated video is scored for physical correctness. PhyGenEval, the accompanying automated evaluation framework, decomposes each video's physical-correctness assessment into three hierarchical sub-tasks/stages: (1) Key Physical Phenomena Detection — single-frame/key-frame visual question answering to confirm the critical physical event appears; (2) Physics Order Verification — multi-image/temporal-sequence checking that key events occur in the physically correct order; (3) Overall Naturalness Evaluation — full-video assessment of whether the dynamics evolve naturally and consistently with physical law. A subset (64 prompts x 8 models = 512 videos) was used for human-correlation validation, with 3 annotators per video scoring on a 0-3 scale.
- **Metrics:** n/r
- **Models evaluated:** 10 T2V models evaluated overall; leaderboard reports 8 main systems with average PCA-based scores (0-1 scale) — closed-source: Gen-3 (Runway) 0.51 (top), Kling 0.49, Pika 0.39; open-source: Vchitect-2.0 (2B) 0.45, CogVideoX-5B 0.45, Open-Sora V1.2 (1.1B) 0.44, CogVideoX-2B 0.39, Lavie (860M) 0.36. Headline ranking: Gen-3 > Kling > (CogVideoX-5B ~ Vchitect-2.0) > Open-Sora V1.2 > (Pika ~ CogVideoX-2B) > Lavie. Even the best model (Gen-3, 0.51) only marginally exceeds the human-baseline reference (~0.48) and remains far from reliable physical correctness, with all models scoring below ~0.51 average.

### 8.8 WorldPrediction: A Benchmark for High-level World Modeling and Long-horizon Procedural Planning
*Meta FAIR (Paris), with HKUST and ISIR / Sorbonne Université. Authors: Delong Chen, Willy Chung, Yejin Bang, Ziwei Ji, Pascale Fung. Released as facebookresearch/WorldPrediction. · 2025-06-04 (arXiv v1 submission, 4 June 2025); accepted at ICML 2025.*  
[arxiv.org/abs/2506.04363](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.04363)

- **Measures:** Tests two high-level abilities of world models on real-world video, deliberately moving beyond the low-level frame/pixel motion prediction emphasized by prior world-model benchmarks. (1) World modeling: whether a model has an internal model of how actions cause state transitions, i.e. can identify which action produced an observed change between an initial and a final world state. (2) Long-horizon procedural planning: whether a model can compose actions into a correctly ordered sequence that transforms an initial state into a goal state. Crucially it targets actions with temporal and semantic abstraction (e.g. food prep, furniture assembly, repair) rather than low-level dynamics. It is grounded in a formal partially-observable semi-Markov Decision Process (semi-MDP / option) framework for reliability and robustness.
- **Tasks:** Two discriminative (multiple-choice over video clips) tasks. WorldPrediction-WM (World Modeling): given visual observations of an initial state and a final state, pick the correct action video that caused the transition out of 4 candidate action clips. WorldPrediction-PP (Procedural Planning): given an initial state and a final goal state, select the correctly ordered sequence of actions from counterfactual candidate sequences. Distractors are built from "action equivalents" — the same/very similar actions drawn from different contexts — so models cannot exploit superficial low-level visual continuity or background cues and must reason about semantics and ordering. Built from real instructional/egocentric video: COIN, CrossTask, IKEA-ASM, EgoExo4D, and EPIC-KITCHENS-100. Sizes: 825 WM samples and 570 PP samples.
- **Metrics:** Multiple-choice selection accuracy (%) — top-1 accuracy at choosing the correct option among 4 candidates, so the random/chance baseline is 25%. Reported separately for WorldPrediction-WM and WorldPrediction-PP. Captures whether a model selects the causally-correct action (WM) or the correctly ordered action sequence (PP) over hard "action-equivalent" distractors. Human performance is reported as the ceiling.
- **Models evaluated:** Broad set across model families. Multimodal LLMs / VLMs: GPT-4o, Gemini-2.0, Claude-3.5-Sonnet, Qwen2.5-VL (72B), Llama-4 Maverick, Llama-3.3 (70B); LLMs operating on captions; feature-matching / representation baselines (e.g. CLIP, V-JEPA / DINO-style encoders); video diffusion models (CogVideoX, I2VGen-XL); and task-specific procedural-planning models (OEPP: Transformer, PDPP). Headline ranking — WM: Qwen2.5-VL-72B best at 57.0%, then Gemini-2.0 55.6%, Llama-4 Maverick 53.6%, Claude-3.5-Sonnet 53.3%, Llama-3.3-70B 52.2%; video diffusion models lagged at ~26-31%. PP: Claude-3.5-Sonnet best at 38.1%, then Qwen2.5-VL-72B 36.7%, Llama-3.3-70B 35.1%, OEPP PDPP 34.4%, OEPP Transformer 34.2%. Humans: 100% on both.
- **Key findings:** Current frontier models are far below humans at high-level/semantic world modeling and planning. The best model reaches only ~57% on WorldPrediction-WM and ~38% on WorldPrediction-PP, versus 100% human accuracy (chance = 25%), so even the leading systems are only modestly above random on planning. No single model dominates: the WM winner (Qwen2.5-VL-72B) is not the PP winner (Claude-3.5-Sonnet). Video diffusion / generative world models underperform discriminative VLMs on these abstract-action tasks. Task-specific planning models (OEPP) are strong only on their in-domain datasets (~49% on COIN/CrossTask) but drop to ~29% out of domain, showing poor generalization. The "action equivalents" distractor design successfully removes low-level shortcut cues, exposing that models lack robust semantic-level world modeling and long-horizon procedural reasoning. Conclusion: today's models, including frontier MLLMs and video generators, do not yet possess reliable high-level world models or planning ability.

### 8.9 EVA / EVA-Bench (Embodied Video Anticipation Benchmark)
*HKUST, Peking University, and collaborators (Xiaowei Chi / litwellchi et al.) · arXiv v1 submitted 2024-10-20 (arXiv:2410.15461); revised v2 2025-06-10 (retitled "Empowering World Models with Reflection for Embodied Video Prediction"); accepted to ICML 2025 (poster, published 2025-05-01).*  
[arxiv.org/abs/2410.15461](https://arxiv.org/abs/2410.15461)

- **Measures:** Embodied world models' ability to anticipate future video from egocentric/robot viewpoints: scene+instruction understanding, action prediction, future-video generation, and task-completion judgment, under multi-step and OOD conditions.
- **Tasks:** Four meta-tasks: Action-Description, How-To, Finish-Thinking, Next-Step, over robot + egocentric scenarios with in-domain and OOD splits; 125 curated eval samples.

### 8.10 VBench-2.0
*Vchitect team — Shanghai AI Laboratory and S-Lab, Nanyang Technological University (NTU). Authors: Dian Zheng, Ziqi Huang (equal contribution), Hongbo Liu, Kai Zou, Yinan He, Fan Zhang, Lulu Gu, Yuanhan Zhang, Jingwen He, Wei-Shi Zheng, Yu Qiao, Ziwei Liu. · arXiv v1 submitted 27 March 2025 (arXiv:2503.21755); revised v2 20 August 2025. Brand-new 2025 benchmark, successor to the original VBench (CVPR 2024).*  
[arxiv.org/abs/2503.21755](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.21755)

- **Measures:** VBench-2.0 evaluates the "intrinsic faithfulness" of video-generation models used as world simulators — i.e. whether generated videos are not merely visually plausible (per-frame quality, smooth motion, basic prompt match, which the original VBench covered) but fundamentally grounded in reality. It tests deeper world-model abilities: adherence to physical laws, commonsense reasoning / causality, anatomical correctness, controllability to instructions, and compositional/creative integrity. Organized into 5 broad ability categories with 18 fine-grained capability dimensions: (1) Human Fidelity, (2) Controllability, (3) Creativity, (4) Physics, (5) Commonsense.
- **Tasks:** Concrete evaluation dimensions (18) across the 5 categories, each with curated prompts/scenarios:
HUMAN FIDELITY (3): Human Anatomy (detect unnatural deformations of hands/faces/bodies); Human Identity (facial-feature consistency over frames); Human Clothes (outfit/temporal consistency over frames).
CONTROLLABILITY (7): Dynamic Spatial Relationship (objects reposition per instruction); Dynamic Attribute (mid-video attribute change, e.g. color); Motion Order Understanding (sequential/ordered actions); Human Interaction (two-person interactions); Complex Landscape (long 150+-word landscape descriptions); Complex Plot (multi-scene narrative consistency); Camera Motion (nine camera-movement types).
CREATIVITY (2): Diversity (inter-sample variation across samples from same prompt); Composition (novel/imaginative species combinations and multi-entity interactions).
PHYSICS (3 state-change + geometry): Mechanics (gravity, buoyancy, stress); Thermotics (vaporization, liquefaction, sublimation); Material (color mixing, hardness, combustion, solubility); Multi-View / 3D Geometry Consistency.
COMMONSENSE (2): Motion Rationality (motions produce expected real-world consequences); Instance Preservation (object counts stay stable over time).
- **Metrics:** Per-dimension "intrinsic faithfulness" scores (normalized 0–1 / percent) over 18 dimensions, aggregated to 5 category scores and an overall score, validated by human-preference annotations with strong Spearman correlation. Computed via a hybrid of VLM/LLM "generalist" judges (video multi-question answering, text-description alignment) and CV "specialist" tools: ViT anomaly detector (anatomy), ArcFace+RetinaFace (identity), CoTracker-v2 (camera motion), VGG-19 features (diversity), SIFT/FLANN/RANSAC + RAFT optical flow (multi-view geometry), and YOLO-World detection (instance preservation).
- **Models evaluated:** Four state-of-the-art text-to-video models are benchmarked in the paper: HunyuanVideo, CogVideoX-1.5, Kling 1.6, and Sora (480p). No single model dominates — abilities are split: Sora and HunyuanVideo lead Human Fidelity and Creativity; Kling 1.6 leads Commonsense and camera-motion controllability; CogVideoX-1.5 is comparatively strong on Physics. All models score moderately overall (roughly 50–62% normalized), leaving large headroom; none is a clear winner across categories.
- **Key findings:** 1) Current SOTA video generators do well on superficial visual quality but struggle with intrinsic faithfulness — they are visually plausible yet not fundamentally realistic. 2) Biggest weaknesses are Controllability dimensions: models fail the large majority (~80%) of simple Dynamic Spatial Relationship and Dynamic Attribute cases (objects that should reposition or change color/attribute mid-video). 3) Complex multi-scene plot generation is largely unsolved; current ~<10s single-shot clips can't sustain coherent narratives. 4) A controllability-vs-creativity trade-off exists: creativity-tuned models lose prompt adherence and vice-versa. 5) Models lack inherent physical understanding, but careful prompt engineering can still elicit physically plausible outcomes — suggesting physics behavior is partly promptable rather than internalized. 6) Physics/commonsense scores are generally low (most models below ~60% on physics tasks), reinforcing that as world models, today's video generators have limited grounding in physical law and causality. Conclusion: VBench and VBench-2.0 are complementary — true world-model quality requires both superficial and intrinsic faithfulness.
- **Sources:** [arxiv.org/abs/2503.21755](https://arxiv.org/abs/2503.21755) · [arxiv.org/html/2503.21755v1](https://arxiv.org/html/2503.21755v1) · [arxiv.org/pdf/2503.21755](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2503.21755) · [vchitect.github.io/VBench-2.0-project](https://vchitect.github.io/VBench-2.0-project/) · [github.com/Vchitect/VBench](https://github.com/Vchitect/VBench) · [huggingface.co/papers/2503.21755](https://huggingface.co/papers/2503.21755)

### 8.11 EWMBench (Embodied World Model Benchmark)
*AgiBot Tech / Zhiyuan Robotics (AgibotTech), with collaborators — authors: Hu Yue, Siyuan Huang, Yue Liao, Shengcong Chen, Pengfei Zhou, Liliang Chen, Maoqing Yao, Guanghui Ren · Released May 14, 2025 (arXiv v1; v2 revised May 18, 2025). Brand-new 2025 benchmark, classified under cs.RO.*  
[arxiv.org/abs/2505.09694](https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.09694)

- **Measures:** EWMBench evaluates "embodied world models" (EWMs) — text/image-to-video generative models used as world models for embodied AI/robot manipulation. Unlike generic video-generation benchmarks (e.g., VBench) that focus on perceptual/aesthetic quality, it assesses whether a model can generate physically plausible, action-consistent rollouts from language commands suitable for robotic policy/planning. It targets three core abilities: (1) visual scene consistency (stable scene structure and coherent viewpoints over time), (2) motion correctness (whether the end-effector/robot-arm trajectory matches the true motion spatially, temporally, and dynamically), and (3) semantic alignment (whether the generated video actually executes the instructed task without commonsense/logical violations). It also measures generation diversity. The dataset is built on AgiBot-World, a large real-world robotic manipulation dataset.
- **Tasks:** 10 robotic manipulation tasks spanning household and industrial contexts, with fixed-viewpoint scenes and end-effector trajectories. Reported tasks include: retrieving toast from a toaster, pouring water, setting cutlery, restocking a freezer, producing ice, factory packing, cleaning bottles, heating food in a microwave, installing a showerhead, and storing objects in a drawer. Curation: ~100 instances per task were sampled and 10 final episodes per task were selected to emphasize trajectory diversity (≈30 evaluated samples/episodes across the 10 tasks). Each episode provides an initial frame + task description, ground-truth video frames, trajectory data (.npy), and gripper-detection annotations; models are evaluated over multiple generated attempts per episode. Stated current limitation: only fixed-viewpoint manipulation; navigation/mobile manipulation planned as extensions.
- **Metrics:** Four dimensions. SCENE: Scene Consistency (SceneC) — fine-tuned DINOv2 patch-level features; cosine similarity between consecutive and initial frames to quantify stable scene structure/viewpoint coherence (authors argue fine-tuned DINOv2 captures layout better and is more viewpoint-sensitive than VBench's DINO-ViT-B/16). MOTION/TRAJECTORY (three complementary metrics on end-effector trajectories): (a) Symmetric Hausdorff Distance (HSD) — spatial alignment / maximum deviation between generated and ground-truth trajectory; (b) Normalized Dynamic Time Warping (NDTW) — spatio-temporal alignment, correct ordering/timing of the motion sequence; (c) Dynamic Consistency (DYN) — motion dynamics (velocity/acceleration distributions) via Wasserstein distance, i.e., smoothness/jitter. SEMANTICS: BLEU (global video caption vs. task instruction), CLIP Score (step-by-step generated descriptions vs. ground truth), and a Logical Error Punishment term (MLLM-based, using Qwen2.5-VL, to detect commonsense/logic violations). DIVERSITY: Semantic Diversity = 1 − CLIP-global-feature similarity. Detector/feature backbones: fine-tuned DINOv2 and YOLO-World (gripper detection), Qwen2.5-VL for semantic analysis. Results are also aggregated into an overall score (~1–5 scale).
- **Models evaluated:** Seven models across three categories, with headline overall ranking (best→worst): 1) EnerVerse_FT (domain-adapted) 4.70; 2) LTX_FT (domain-adapted) 4.55; 3) Kling-1.6 (commercial) 3.87; 4) Hailuo I2V-01-live (commercial) 3.41; 5) COSMOS-7B (open-source) 3.29; 6) OpenSora 2.0 (open-source) 3.14; 7) LTX (open-source, zero-shot) 2.97. Domain-adapted/fine-tuned models consistently beat commercial and open-source zero-shot models, especially on semantics and dynamics.
- **Key findings:** 1) Domain adaptation dominates: task-specific fine-tuning (EnerVerse_FT, LTX_FT) markedly improves task understanding and motion alignment over commercial/open-source zero-shot models. 2) The three motion metrics are complementary and non-redundant: HSD flags spatial errors, NDTW flags temporal sequencing/timing errors, DYN flags motion smoothness/jitter. 3) EWMBench rankings align more closely with human judgment than VBench (human evaluation aggregated over models; explicit Spearman/Kendall coefficients are not stated in the paper text). 4) Characteristic model failures: LTX/COSMOS hallucinate human hands instead of the robot arm; OpenSora produces jittery arm motion; Hailuo renders cartoon-like scenes; even domain-adapted models sometimes show "empty grasping" (arm completes motion without actually grasping the object). 5) Overall conclusion: current video-generation-based world models still have significant gaps in physical plausibility and task coherence, and perceptual-quality benchmarks are insufficient — EWMBench is positioned to expose these limitations and guide next-generation embodied models.
- **Sources:** [arxiv.org/abs/2505.09694](https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.09694) · [arxiv.org/html/2505.09694v2](https://arxiv.org/html/2505.09694v2) · [github.com/AgibotTech/EWMBench](https://github.com/AgibotTech/EWMBench) · [www.emergentmind.com/topics/embodied-world-model-benchmark-ewmbench](https://www.emergentmind.com/topics/embodied-world-model-benchmark-ewmbench) · [www.themoonlight.io/en/review/ewmbench-evaluating-scene-motion-and-semantic-quality-in-embodied-world-models](https://www.themoonlight.io/en/review/ewmbench-evaluating-scene-motion-and-semantic-quality-in-embodied-world-models)

### 8.12 WorldTest / AutumnBench (Benchmarking World-Model Learning with Environment-Level Queries)
*Authors affiliated with MIT (Joshua B. Tenenbaum), Cornell (Kevin Ellis), Basis Research Institute (Zenna Tavares), plus collaborators (Mila/DeepMind/DFKI). Full author list: Archana Warrier, Dat Nguyen, Michelangelo Naim, Moksh Jain, Yichao Liang, Karen Schroeder, Cambridge Yang, Joshua B. Tenenbaum, Sebastian Vollmer, Kevin Ellis, Zenna Tavares. · First submitted to arXiv October 22, 2025 (v1). Latest version v4 dated May 7, 2026. Brand-new 2025 benchmark.*  
[arxiv.org/abs/2510.19788](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.19788)

- **Measures:** Whether agents actually LEARN a world model that supports many different, unanticipated queries about an environment, rather than just optimizing one reward. Two core abilities are stressed: (1) understanding the global/causal structure of the environment's dynamics, and (2) reasoning about counterfactual consequences. Crucially it tests the learning process itself: active exploration (gathering the right information via interaction, including using resets as hypothesis-testing tools) and belief updating (revising one's model when confronted with contradictory evidence). WorldTest is the general protocol: it separates a reward-free interaction phase (agent freely explores a base environment, can reset at will, no task given) from a later scored test phase in a modified-but-related challenge environment with explicit objectives. It is open-ended (tasks are unknown ahead of time) and representation-agnostic (scores behavior only, so any model architecture can be compared).
- **Tasks:** Instantiated as AutumnBench: 43 interactive grid-world environments (built in the Autumn DSL of causal/dynamic programs) yielding 129 tasks across three query families. (1) Masked Frame Prediction (MFP): the agent observes a trajectory with partially masked frames and must predict the missing content of the final frame by choosing from six options. (2) Planning: the agent must generate a sequence of actions to reach a specified target state. (3) Change Detection (CD): the agent identifies that the environment's underlying dynamics have changed and reports the earliest timestep at which the observation diverges from what its model expected. Each is preceded by free reward-free exploration of the environment.
- **Metrics:** Per-task scoring: Masked Frame Prediction and Planning use binary accuracy (1 if correct / target state reached, 0 otherwise). Change Detection uses a dynamic/graduated penalty function: full credit for detecting the change exactly on time or one step early, with smooth (increasingly large) penalties for late detection. Aggregate performance is reported as a normalized score across the 129 tasks; humans are summarized at an 80th-percentile aggregate. The paper also reports behavioral diagnostics as metrics of the learning process: fraction of actions that are resets (a proxy for hypothesis-testing exploration: ~12.5% for humans vs <7% for models) and belief-updating behavior under contradictory evidence.
- **Models evaluated:** 517 human participants plus three frontier reasoning models: OpenAI o3, Anthropic Claude (Claude 4 Sonnet), and Google Gemini 2.5 Pro. Headline ranking: humans substantially outperform all three models across all environments; no model approaches human performance. The reasoning models cluster well below the human aggregate (80th-percentile humans set the top reference).
- **Key findings:** Current frontier models are far from human-level at learning world models: humans beat all evaluated reasoning models on every environment. The gap is explained primarily by differences in the learning process rather than raw capability: (1) Exploration — humans actively use resets as hypothesis-testing tools (~12.5% of actions) whereas models reset rarely (<7%) and explore less purposefully; (2) Belief updating — models often fail to revise their internal model when shown evidence that contradicts it. Scaling test-time compute helps unevenly: more compute improved performance in 25 of 43 environments but was flat or worse in the other 18, so compute scaling is not a reliable path to closing the gap. Conclusion: building agents that explore efficiently and update beliefs from contradictory evidence — not just larger models — is the key open problem for world-model learning.
- **Sources:** [arxiv.org/abs/2510.19788](https://arxiv.org/abs/2510.19788) · [arxiv.org/html/2510.19788v1](https://arxiv.org/html/2510.19788v1) · [arxiv.org/pdf/2510.19788](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2510.19788) · [openreview.net/forum?id=HuNIgYhBoy](https://openreview.net/forum?id=HuNIgYhBoy) · [autumn.basis.ai](https://autumn.basis.ai)

# Defining "Spatial World Model" / "3D World Model" (2024–2026)

A study-report synthesis of how the terms are defined across recent literature and industry. Verified claims are cited inline; items I could not fully confirm from primary sources are flagged **[UNVERIFIED]** or **[INDUSTRY CLAIM]**.

---

## (a) Definitions and Distinctions

### Common definition
There is no single canonical definition, but a convergent one across industry and surveys is:

> A **spatial / 3D world model** is a generative model that produces a *persistent, geometrically-consistent, explorable 3D representation* of an environment from partial inputs (text, image(s), video, panorama, or coarse layout), and lets humans or agents interact with that environment.

World Labs frames world models around three core functions — to **"reconstruct, generate, and simulate 3D worlds; and allow both humans and agents to interact with them"** — and describes the goal as lifting "whatever input signals are available into a full 3D world" and iteratively updating that understanding ([World Labs, Marble blog](https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/marble-world-model)). This is positioned under the broader banner of **"spatial intelligence,"** which World Labs calls "the next frontier in AI" requiring "powerful world models" ([World Labs](https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/marble-world-model); [Fast Company](https://www.fastcompany.com/91437004/fei-fei-li-world-labs-spatial-ai-mapping-3d)).

The defining attributes that recur in the literature:
- **3D structure** — an explicit or geometry-grounded scene representation (Gaussian splats, NeRF, meshes, or pointmaps), not just pixels.
- **Geometric / physical consistency** — surfaces, depth, and layout remain coherent across viewpoints.
- **Persistence** — the world does not "forget" regions when they leave view; revisiting yields the same scene.
- **Interactivity / explorability** — a user or agent can navigate, and (in stronger forms) edit or act on the world.

A useful broad survey context: *Understanding World or Predicting Future? A Comprehensive Survey of World Models* ([arXiv:2411.14499](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2411.14499)) and *A Comprehensive Survey on World Models for Embodied AI* ([arXiv:2510.16732](https://arxiv.org/html/2510.16732v1)) both organize world models along a spectrum from latent/representation prediction to full environment simulation.

### (i) vs. RL latent world models (Dreamer / MuZero)

| | RL latent world models | Spatial / 3D world models |
|---|---|---|
| Representation | Compact **learned latent state**; no explicit geometry | **Explicit 3D geometry** (splats/NeRF/mesh/pointmaps) |
| Purpose | Planning / policy learning ("imagination" rollouts) for control | Generating navigable, persistent worlds for viewing, editing, simulation |
| Output | Predicted reward/value/next-latent (MuZero) or reconstructed latent rollouts (Dreamer) | A 3D scene a human/agent can explore |
| Spatial fidelity | Not required; latent need not be human-interpretable | Central requirement |

Dreamer learns a latent dynamics model and unrolls trajectories *in latent space* ("latent imagination") to train a policy; MuZero learns an **implicit** model predicting only decision-relevant quantities (reward, policy, value) rather than reconstructing observations ([Dreamer v2 / Hafner et al.](https://gbarnier.github.io/papers/dreamer2.pdf); [deep RL course notes](https://deeprlcourse.github.io/course_notes/model-based/)). The key contrast: latent RL world models are **task/control-oriented and need no 3D structure**, whereas spatial world models make **explicit 3D geometry and explorability the deliverable**.

### (ii) vs. 2D video world models (Sora / Genie)

Video world models generate frames autoregressively or via diffusion and are powerful "simulators," but their world lives in **pixel/frame space, not an explicit 3D representation**.

- DeepMind explicitly contrasts Genie 3 with 3D methods: Genie 3's worlds are "created **frame by frame**" rather than relying on "an **explicit 3D representation**" like NeRFs or Gaussian Splatting ([DeepMind, Genie 3](https://deepmind.google/blog/genie-3-a-new-frontier-for-world-models/)). It is "a general purpose world model that can generate … interactive environments" navigable "in real time at **24 frames per second**" (720p), with "**visual memory extending as far back as one minute ago**" and consistency "for several minutes."
- The persistence weakness is the defining gap: because of "limited temporal context window sizes," video world models "maintain scene consistency during revisits" poorly, "leading to **severe forgetting** of previously generated environments" ([*Video World Models with Long-term Spatial Memory*, arXiv:2506.05284](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.05284)). The fix proposed there is to bolt on a **"geometry-grounded long-term spatial memory"** — i.e., an explicit 3D store — which is precisely the boundary between a pure video world model and a spatial one.

**Summary distinction:** Sora/Genie-style models = high-fidelity, *temporally* coherent **2D frame** simulators with bounded memory; spatial/3D world models = *geometrically* coherent, **persistent 3D** scenes that survive revisits and (often) can be exported/edited. World Labs positions Marble's persistent, downloadable 3D output explicitly against competitors "who have focused on real-time streamed video" ([Radiance Fields](https://radiancefields.com/world-labs-formally-launches-marble-a-generative-world-model); [PYMNTS](https://www.pymnts.com/artificial-intelligence-2/2025/world-labs-launches-marble-as-spatial-intelligence-becomes-the-new-ai-battleground/)). **[INDUSTRY CLAIM — competitive framing, not a neutral technical fact]**

The boundary is **converging**: video models increasingly add 3D memory ([arXiv:2506.05284](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.05284); *Geometry Forcing*, [arXiv:2507.07982](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.07982v1)), so the "spatial vs. video" line is best read as a **spectrum of how explicit the 3D representation is**, not a hard dichotomy.

## (b) Main technical families for spatial world models

### 1. Feed-forward 3D generation (Gaussian splats / NeRF / meshes)
A single network maps input view(s) directly to a 3D representation in one forward pass — no per-scene optimization.
- **AnySplat** — feed-forward 3D Gaussian Splatting from uncalibrated, unposed images, predicting geometry/poses "in one shot" ([arXiv:2505.23716](https://arxiv.org/abs/2505.23716)).
- **F3D-Gaus** — feed-forward 3D-aware generation on ImageNet with cycle-aggregative Gaussian splatting for cross-view consistency ([arXiv:2501.06714](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2501.06714)).
- **Marble (World Labs)** — generates persistent 3D worlds exportable as **Gaussian splats, meshes, or videos**, with AI-native editing ([World Labs blog](https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/marble-world-model); [bigger-better-worlds](https://www.worldlabs.ai/blog/bigger-better-worlds)). Internally uses "millions of 3D gaussians" rather than polygons. Beta Sept 16 2025; GA Nov 12 2025. **[Dates per secondary sources: [AI Wiki](https://aiwiki.ai/wiki/marble_world_labs), [Radiance Fields](https://radiancefields.com/world-labs-formally-launches-marble-a-generative-world-model) — not independently confirmed against a World Labs press release.]**

### 2. Autoregressive video-to-3D (streaming geometry)
Models that ingest a video/image stream and maintain a continuously-updated 3D state, predicting pointmaps/geometry online.
- **CUT3R — Continuous 3D Perception Model with Persistent State** — recurrent transformer predicting pointmaps from a stream, with a persistent internal state that can probe unseen views ([arXiv:2501.12387](https://arxiv.org/abs/2501.12387)).
- **VGGT — Visual Geometry Grounded Transformer** (CVPR 2025) — feed-forward joint prediction of cameras/depth/geometry from many views ([referenced in embodied-WM survey repo](https://github.com/tsinghua-fib-lab/Awesome-Embodied-World-Model)). **[Venue "CVPR 2025" per survey listing; verify against the paper if cited formally.]**
- **LongStream** — online, future-invisible streaming visual geometry, metric-scale over thousands of frames at ~18 FPS ([arXiv:2602.13172](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2602.13172)). **[arXiv ID format (2602.xxxxx) implies Feb 2026; treat as very recent/preprint — confirm before formal citation.]**
- **tttLRM** — test-time-training for long-context autoregressive 3D reconstruction ([arXiv:2602.20160](https://arxiv.org/pdf/2602.20160)). **[Same 2602 caveat.]**

### 3. Diffusion-based scene generation
Iterative outpainting/inpainting + depth/diffusion priors to grow an explorable 3D scene from text or one image.
- **WonderWorld** — interactive 3D scene generation from a single image ([arXiv:2406.09394](https://arxiv.org/html/2406.09394v1)).
- **WonderJourney** — single image → sequence of point clouds via outpainting + unprojection (slow: noted ~700s/scene) ([described in WonderWorld](https://arxiv.org/html/2406.09394v1)).
- **LucidDreamer** — single image → multi-view synthesis → trained 3DGS; "navigable," "expandable" scenes ([discussed in WonderWorld](https://arxiv.org/html/2406.09394v1)).
- **RealmDreamer** — text-driven 3D scene generation with inpainting + depth diffusion ([arXiv:2404.07199](https://arxiv.org/html/2404.07199v1)).
- **HoloDreamer** — holistic 3D panoramic world from text ([arXiv:2407.15187](https://arxiv.org/html/2407.15187v1)).

### 4. Real-time frame (interactive) world models
Autoregressive frame generators that respond to user actions in real time. These are the *video-world-model* end of the spectrum but are central to "interactive world model" framing; spatial fidelity/persistence is their main limitation.
- **Genie 3 (DeepMind)** — real-time navigable worlds, 720p/24fps, minute-scale visual memory; **no explicit 3D representation** ([DeepMind](https://deepmind.google/blog/genie-3-a-new-frontier-for-world-models/)). Limited research preview. **[Public availability per secondary sources.]**
- **Oasis (Decart)** — real-time, end-to-end transformer-generated playable Minecraft-like world at ~20 fps, frame-by-frame from keyboard/mouse input ([Decart publication](https://decart.ai/publications/oasis-interactive-ai-video-game-model); [TechCrunch, Oct 2024](https://techcrunch.com/2024/10/31/decarts-ai-simulates-a-real-time-playable-version-of-minecraft/)).
- **Odyssey** — streams interactive 3D worlds up to ~30 fps on H100 clusters, reported "$1–$2 per user-hour" ([TechCrunch, May 2025](https://techcrunch.com/2025/05/28/odysseys-new-ai-model-streams-3d-interactive-worlds/)). **[Cost/fps figures are reporter-stated, not from a primary technical report.]**

### Cross-cutting trend (families 3↔4 converging into "spatial")
Video world models are acquiring explicit 3D memory to gain persistence — e.g., *Video World Models with Long-term Spatial Memory* ([arXiv:2506.05284](https://arxiv.org/abs/2506.05284)) and *Geometry Forcing* which "marries video diffusion and 3D representation for consistent world modeling" ([arXiv:2507.07982](https://arxiv.org/html/2507.07982v1)). This is the strongest signal that "spatial world model" is being defined precisely by the **addition of persistent, geometry-grounded 3D state** to otherwise-2D generative video models.
